Chapter 07Particles -- The 1:1 Map

Memory Technique Spotlight: Analogical Transfer + Elaborative Interrogation

Particles transfer almost directly from Korean (은/는→は, 이/가→が, 을/를→を). By asking "WHY does this particle work the same way?" for each mapping, you create deep elaborative connections that make forgetting structurally difficult.

Palace Assignment: The Bathroom (화장실)

11 particles, 11 bathroom fixtures. は on the mirror (you look at yourself — YOU are the topic), が on the towel hook (it GRABS), を on the toilet paper (WRAPPED around), に on the tile grout (NEEDLE-thin), で at the shower (where you DO things), へ at the door (going TOWARD), の at the drain (spiral connecting), から at the faucet (comes FROM), まで at the bathtub (fill UNTIL the line), も at the toothbrush holder (MOre!), と at the double sink (TIED together).

In Chapter 05, you said これをお願いします -- "this-を, please." You said 駅はどこですか -- "station-は, where?" You were already using particles. Now let's understand what they are and how perfectly they map to Korean.

"Korean speakers don't need to learn what particles ARE -- you already use them every sentence. You just need to learn the Japanese equivalents. And the mapping is almost 1:1."

The Core Particle Map

Here is the complete Korean-to-Japanese particle map. Every Korean particle you know has a Japanese counterpart. The mnemonic column gives you a memory hook for each one.

FunctionKoreanJapaneseMnemonic
Topic 은/는 は (wa) "HA!" -- THIS is the topic!
Subject 이/가 が (ga) が GRABS the subject
Object 을/를 を (wo) を WRAPS the object
Location (exist) 에 に (ni) に NEEDLES into a point
Location (action) 에서 で (de) で = DOING particle
Direction 으로/로 へ (e) へ = hill going toward
Possessive 의 の (no) の spirals connecting
From 에서 から (kara) から CUTS away
Until 까지 まで (made) MAkes the DEadline
Also 도 も (mo) MOre -- me too!
And (nouns) 와/과 と (to) と TIES together
Korean and Japanese particles map nearly 1:1. Each line connects equivalent functions. Where you know one, you already know the other.
Elaborative Interrogation

WHY do Korean and Japanese share an almost identical particle system when their core vocabularies are so different?

Think about this before reading on. Particles mark grammatical relationships -- topic, subject, object, location, direction. Both languages evolved as SOV (Subject-Object-Verb) agglutinative languages, likely through centuries of structural convergence in the same geographic sphere. The particle system is the grammatical skeleton, and both languages built the same skeleton independently (or through deep contact). Vocabulary is the "flesh" on that skeleton -- and each language grew its own flesh from different roots. This is why your grammar intuition transfers perfectly even when you need entirely new words.

Particles in Action -- With Korean Parallels

Let's see each particle at work. For every example, notice how the particle occupies the exact same structural position in both languages.

は (wa) -- Topic Marker = 은/는

Korean

나는 학생이다

na-neun haksaeng-ida

Japanese

私は学生だ

watashi wa gakusei da

Function: Marks what we're talking ABOUT. "As for me... (I'm a student)." Identical logic in both languages.

が (ga) -- Subject Marker = 이/가

Korean

비가 온다

bi-ga onda

Japanese

雨が降る

ame ga furu

Function: Identifies the doer/subject. "Rain (subject) falls." Both languages even use the same particle sound: 가/が!

を (wo) -- Object Marker = 을/를

Korean

커피를 마신다

keopi-reul masinda

Japanese

コーヒーを飲む

koohii wo nomu

Function: Marks the object receiving the action. "Coffee (object) drink." Same slot, same purpose.

に (ni) -- Location/Target = 에

Korean

학교에 간다

hakgyo-e ganda

Japanese

学校に行く

gakkou ni iku

Function: Points to a destination or location where something exists. "School (to/at) go."

で (de) -- Action Location = 에서

Korean

도서관에서 공부한다

doseogwan-eseo gongbuhanda

Japanese

図書館で勉強する

toshokan de benkyou suru

Function: Marks where an action HAPPENS. "Library (at) study." Remember: で = DOING things there.

の (no) -- Possessive = 의

Korean

나의 책

na-ui chaek

Japanese

私の本

watashi no hon

Function: Connects possessor to possessed. "My book." Think of の as a spiral that ties two things together.

Confusion Zones -- Where Korean and Japanese Diverge

The particle map is almost 1:1 -- but not quite. Here are the three areas where one Japanese particle covers multiple Korean particles, and vice versa. These are your real danger zones.

Confusion Zone 1: に covers 에 + 에게 + 한테

In Korean, you use different particles for location (에), giving to a person (에게), and informal "to someone" (한테). In Japanese, に does all three.

KoreanJapaneseExample
학교에 간다 学校に行く Go to school (location)
친구에게 준다 友達にあげる Give to a friend (recipient)
선생님한테 말한다 先生に話す Talk to the teacher (target person)

Rule: When pointing at a location, a time, a recipient, or a target -- use に. It's simpler than Korean here.

Confusion Zone 2: で covers 에서 + (으)로

Japanese で handles both the "place where action happens" (에서) AND "by means of" ((으)로). Two Korean particles, one Japanese particle.

KoreanJapaneseExample
도서관에서 공부한다 図書館で勉強する Study at the library (action location)
버스로 간다 バスで行く Go by bus (means/tool)
일본어로 말한다 日本語で話す Speak in Japanese (medium)

Rule: で = "DOING" particle. Where you're DOING something, or the tool you're DOING it with. Both concepts involve action.

Confusion Zone 3: から covers 에서 + 부터

Korean distinguishes between "from a place" (에서) and "from a time/starting point" (부터). Japanese uses から for both.

KoreanJapaneseExample
서울에서 왔다 ソウルから来た Came from Seoul (place origin)
3시부터 시작 3時から始まる Starts from 3 o'clock (time origin)

Rule: から = "CUTS away from the starting point." Whether that starting point is a place or a time, it's always から.

Summary of Confusion Zones

Japanese is actually simpler than Korean here. Where Korean has multiple particles for fine distinctions, Japanese uses fewer particles that cover broader ranges. This is good news: fewer particles to learn, and when in doubt, the broader Japanese particle is more likely to be correct.

The は vs が Distinction -- Your Korean Advantage

This is THE most famous puzzle in Japanese grammar. Textbooks for English speakers devote entire chapters to explaining this distinction -- but you already understand it because Korean has the exact same split: 은/는 vs 이/가.

は = "As for..." (Topic -- old/known information)

田中さんは先生です

"As for Tanaka, (he) is a teacher."

다나카 씨는 선생님이에요

Same logic: "As for Tanaka..."

が = "It is... that..." (Subject -- new/important information)

田中さんが先生です

"Tanaka is the one who is the teacher." (Not someone else!)

다나카 씨가 선생님이에요

Same logic: "It's Tanaka who..."

The Quick Test (Works in Both Languages)

SituationUse...KoreanJapanese
Introducing a topic ("speaking of X...") は / 는 날씨는 좋다 天気はいい
Answering "who/what?" (identifying) が / 가 누구가 왔어? 誰が来た？
Contrast ("X, on the other hand...") は / 는 이것은 좋지만... これはいいけど...
Existence/natural phenomena が / 가 비가 온다 雨が降る
Expressing ability/desire (after が) が / 가 일본어가 하고 싶다 日本語が話したい

Bottom line: If your Korean instinct says 는, use は. If it says 가, use が. Your instinct is right ~95% of the time.

Compound Particles -- Stacking for Precision

Just like Korean stacks particles (에는, 에서는, 에서의), Japanese stacks particles too. The logic is identical.

CompoundComponentsKorean ParallelExample
には に (location) + は (topic) 에는 日本には四季がある = In Japan (as for), there are four seasons
では で (action location) + は (topic) 에서는 学校では英語を話す = At school (as for), we speak English
からの から (from) + の (possessive) 에서의 / 로부터의 友達からの手紙 = A letter from a friend
までに まで (until) + に (time point) 까지에 5時までに来てください = Please come by 5 o'clock
にも に (location) + も (also) 에도 東京にも行った = I went to Tokyo, too
でも で (means) + も (also) 에서도 / (이)라도 コーヒーでも飲もうか = Shall we have coffee or something?
との と (with) + の (possessive) 와의 / 과의 友達との約束 = A promise with a friend
The Stacking Rule

Compound particles follow the same logic in both languages: specific particle first, then は/も/の for additional nuance. If you can stack 에서는 in Korean, you can stack では in Japanese. The order and logic are identical.
