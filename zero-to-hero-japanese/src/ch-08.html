Chapter 08Verb Conjugation -- Same System, Different Clothes

Memory Technique Spotlight: Procedural Memory + Pattern Completion

Verb conjugation is procedural knowledge — like riding a bicycle. Once the pattern is drilled enough, it becomes automatic. You'll practice until conjugation feels like muscle memory, not conscious thinking. The te-form song ("U-TSU-RU → って, MU-BU-NU → んで, KU → いて, GU → いで, SU → して") is your procedural anchor.

Korean and Japanese both conjugate verbs by stacking suffixes onto stems. You already understand this system intuitively -- in Korean, 먹다 becomes 먹습니다, 먹었다, 먹어서, 먹을 수 있다. Japanese does the exact same thing, just with different endings.

"You know how to drive. Now the controls are rearranged."
The Big Idea

In Korean, you never think twice about conjugation mechanics -- you just do it. The agglutinative system (stacking endings) is already in your muscle memory. This chapter maps Japanese conjugation forms onto your existing Korean framework. You are not learning a new system; you are re-labeling the controls.

Anchor Verb: 食べる (taberu) = 먹다 (to eat)

We start with one verb and master every form. Once you see the full pattern on a single verb, every other verb becomes a variation. Think of 食べる as your Rosetta Stone.

Pattern Recognition

Notice the stem 食べ (tabe) stays constant in every form. The endings change: -る, -ます, -ない, -た, -て, -られる, -よう, -れば, -たら. This is identical to how Korean keeps 먹 as the stem and stacks: -다, -습니다, -지 않다, -었다, -어서, -을 수 있다, -자, -으면. Same engine, different fuel.

The 食べ stem is the hub. Every conjugation is just a different suffix -- exactly like Korean 먹- with its endings.

The Three Verb Groups

Japanese verbs fall into three groups, each with its own conjugation rules. Korean does not make this same distinction, so this is one area where you are learning something genuinely new. The good news: once you identify the group, conjugation is completely predictable.

Group 2 -- Ichidan (一段): "One Step"

Rule: Drop る and add the ending. That is it. One step.

Identifier: The verb ends in -いる or -える (with some exceptions).

Korean parallel: Think of Korean verbs where the stem never changes -- like 가다 → 가요, 갑니다, 갔다. The 가 stays put. Ichidan verbs work the same way.

VerbMeaningStemPoliteNegativeTe-form
食べるto eat食べ 食べます食べない食べて
見るto see/watch見 見ます見ない見て
起きるto get up起き 起きます起きない起きて
教えるto teach教え 教えます教えない教えて
Mnemonic

"Ichidan = One step, One cut." You make ONE cut (drop る), then paste the new ending. No stem changes, no tricks. This is the easiest group.

Group 1 -- Godan (五段): "Five Steps"

Rule: The final consonant of the stem shifts across the five vowel rows (あ段, い段, う段, え段, お段). Hence "five steps."

Identifier: Most verbs that do NOT end in -いる or -える. Also, some -いる/-える verbs are actually godan (tricky!).

Godan verbs "walk" across the five vowel rows. Once you see the pattern, it becomes automatic.

Group 3 -- Irregular: Just Two Verbs

There are only two irregular verbs in Japanese. That is it. Just two. Memorize them and move on.

Formする (to do)Korean来る/くる (to come)Korean

Politeします합니다来ます (きます)옵니다

Korean Connection

する is the Japanese 하다. Just like Korean attaches 하다 to nouns to make verbs (勉強하다 = to study), Japanese attaches する: 勉強する (benkyou suru). The pattern is identical. Notice also that 来る changes its reading: くる → きます → こない. The kanji stays, but the reading shifts. This is the only truly unpredictable part.

Elaborative Interrogation

WHY does Japanese have only 2 irregular verbs while Korean has more irregular patterns (ㅂ-irregular, ㄷ-irregular, ㅎ-irregular, etc.)?

Japanese irregularity concentrates in just する and 来る because the language historically regularized most verbs into the godan/ichidan system. Korean retained more stem-changing patterns from Middle Korean. For you, this is good news -- Japanese irregularity is far simpler than what you already handle in Korean.

Te-Form: The Swiss Army Knife of Japanese Conjugation

The te-form is the single most important conjugation to master. It connects sentences, makes requests, describes ongoing actions, gives permission, and more. For ichidan and irregular verbs, it is simple. For godan verbs, the ending depends on the dictionary form's final kana.

Te-Form Rules for Godan Verbs

Dictionary EndingTe-Form ChangeExampleTe-FormMnemonic Group
う, つ, る → って 買う, 待つ, 帰る 買って, 待って, 帰って U-TSU-RU
む, ぶ, ぬ → んで 飲む, 遊ぶ, 死ぬ 飲んで, 遊んで, 死んで M-B-N
く → いて 書く 書いて K
ぐ → いで 泳ぐ 泳いで G
す → して 話す 話して S
Exception Alert

行く (iku = to go) is the one godan verb that breaks the K rule. Instead of 行いて, it becomes 行って (itte). Just memorize this single exception.

The Te-Form Song -- Memory Aid

The Te-Form Song

う、つ、る → って
む、ぶ、ぬ → んで
く → いて    ぐ → いで
す → して

Sing it, chant it, tattoo it. This pattern covers every single godan verb's te-form. Repeat until automatic.

Rhythm mnemonic: "U-tsu-ru って! Mu-bu-nu んで! Ku いて, gu いで, su して!" (Clap on each arrow.)

The Te-Form Map. Five groups for godan verbs, one rule for ichidan, two memorized irregulars. That covers every verb in Japanese.

Practical Connection: Now You Know WHY

In Chapter 05, you used survival phrases as complete units without knowing the grammar behind them. Now you can see the machinery.

Decoding Ch05 Phrases with Te-Form

止めてください

tomete kudasai = "Stop, please"

止める (ichidan, "to stop") → drop る → 止め + て = 止めて
+ ください = "please"

세우다 → 세워 + 주세요 = 세워주세요 -- same pattern!

曲がってください

magatte kudasai = "Turn, please"

曲がる (godan, U-TSU-RU group) → る → って = 曲がって
+ ください = "please"

돌다 → 돌아 + 주세요 = 돌아주세요

見せてください

misete kudasai = "Show me, please"

見せる (ichidan) → drop る → 見せ + て = 見せて
+ ください = "please"

書いてください

kaite kudasai = "Write it, please"

書く (godan, K group) → く → いて = 書いて
+ ください = "please"

Te-form + ください = Polite request. This is the #1 practical use of te-form, and you have been using it since Chapter 05.

The Universal Request Formula

[Verb te-form] + ください = "Please [verb]." This maps perfectly to Korean [verb 해/아/어 form] + 주세요. The structure is identical -- only the specific forms differ.

Adjective Conjugation: Yes, Adjectives Conjugate Too

In Korean, adjectives conjugate like verbs (덥다 → 더워요 → 더웠어요). Japanese does the same thing -- but splits adjectives into two distinct types that follow different rules.

い-Adjectives: They HAVE the い

Rule: Drop the final い and add the ending.

Form暑い (atsui = hot)高い (takai = expensive/tall)Korean Parallel
Present暑い高い덥다 / 비싸다
Negative暑くない高くない덥지 않다 / 비싸지 않다
Past暑かった高かった더웠다 / 비쌌다
Past Negative暑くなかった高くなかった덥지 않았다
Te-form暑くて高くて더워서 / 비싸서
Adverb暑く高く덥게 / 비싸게
Exception: いい (good)

いい (good) is irregular. Its conjugated forms use よ- as the stem: よくない (not good), よかった (was good), よくなかった (was not good). The dictionary form is いい, but everything else uses よ-.

な-Adjectives: They NEED な

Rule: They act like nouns. Add な before nouns, and use だ/じゃない/だった/じゃなかった for conjugation.

Form静か (shizuka = quiet)元気 (genki = healthy/well)Korean Parallel
Present静かだ元気だ조용하다 / 건강하다
Before noun静かな部屋元気な人조용한 방 / 건강한 사람
Negative静かじゃない元気じゃない조용하지 않다

Past Negative静かじゃなかった元気じゃなかった조용하지 않았다
Te-form静かで元気で조용해서 / 조용하고

Mnemonic: い vs. な

暑い

い-adjectives HAVE the い.

The い is built-in. You conjugate by replacing it.

静かな

な-adjectives NEED な.

They are missing something -- they need な to modify nouns.

"HAVE い" vs. "NEED な" -- this one mnemonic sorts every adjective.

Two adjective types, two patterns. い-adjectives conjugate internally; な-adjectives use noun-style endings. Korean speakers: な-adjectives are like 하다-adjectives (조용하다, 건강하다).
Elaborative Interrogation

WHY does Japanese split adjectives into two groups while Korean uses a unified adjective conjugation system?

Japanese い-adjectives descend from Old Japanese native adjectives (形容詞), while な-adjectives evolved from nouns combined with the copula (形容動詞). Korean adjectives all conjugate verb-style regardless of origin. The split in Japanese is a historical artifact -- but for you, notice that な-adjectives work almost identically to Korean 하다-adjectives (조용하다 = 静かだ). The mapping is tighter than it first appears.
