Chapter 17Cultural Pragmatics — What the Textbook Won't Teach You

Memory Technique Spotlight: Cultural Schema + Narrative Encoding

Cultural knowledge is best remembered as stories, not rules. Each cultural concept in this chapter is presented as a narrative — a scenario you can mentally inhabit. These cultural schemas then guide your behavior automatically in real Japanese social situations.

In Ch05, you learned to say ごちそうさまでした after a meal. But WHY? Why do you announce you're about to eat (いただきます)? Why do you apologize when someone gives you a gift? This chapter explains the cultural logic behind the language — the unwritten rules that separate "correct Japanese" from "natural Japanese."

"Grammar gets you understood. Cultural pragmatics gets you accepted. Korean and Japanese cultures are cousins — close enough to feel familiar, different enough to cause real misunderstandings."

Section 1: 空気を読む (Read the Air) ↔ 눈치 (Social Awareness)

Both Korean and Japanese cultures value the ability to perceive unspoken social cues. But they execute this skill differently.

Korean: 눈치

  • Active social intelligence
  • You notice and react quickly
  • Can be direct once you've read the situation
  • 눈치가 빠르다 = "quick to catch on" (compliment)
  • 눈치가 없다 = "clueless" (insult)
↔

Japanese: 空気を読む

  • Passive social harmony
  • You perceive and adjust silently
  • Remain indirect even after reading the room
  • 空気が読める = "can read the air" (basic expectation)
  • KY (空気読めない) = "can't read the air" (social death)
The Critical Difference

In Korea, once you've read the room, you can act — sometimes bluntly. In Japan, reading the room means adjusting your behavior to avoid disruption, often without ever directly addressing the issue. Korean 눈치 is a tool for action. Japanese 空気を読む is a tool for non-action.

Practical Example: Overtime at Work

Korean Workplace

Boss is still working. You notice (눈치). You might stay, or you might say "먼저 들어가겠습니다" (I'll head out first) — direct acknowledgment.

Japanese Workplace

Boss is still working. You read the air. You stay silently. If you must leave, you say お先に失礼します (Excuse me for leaving first) with apologetic tone, possibly bowing.

Section 2: 本音と建前 (True Feelings vs Public Face) ↔ 겉과 속

Every culture has a gap between what people think and what they say. In Japan, this gap has a name and a system.

Korean and Japanese both soften refusals, but Japanese takes indirectness further. When a Japanese person says ちょっと... (a bit...) and trails off, they are saying "no." This is 建前 in action.

Decoding Indirect Japanese

They SayThey MeanKorean Equivalent
ちょっと... No (polite refusal) 좀...
考えておきます Probably no (I'll "think about it") 생각해 볼게요 (but in Korean this might actually mean they'll think about it)
難しいですね No, it's impossible 어렵겠네요 (Korean: genuinely difficult; Japanese: flat refusal)
前向きに検討します We will not do this (business euphemism) 긍정적으로 검토하겠습니다 (Korean might actually mean it)
結構です No thank you (can also mean "fine/sufficient") 괜찮습니다 (same ambiguity exists in Korean)
いいです Context-dependent: "Yes, good" or "No, I'm fine (don't need it)" 괜찮아요 (same ambiguity)
Elaborative Interrogation

WHY is Japanese indirectness stronger than Korean?

Consider the cultural concept of 和 (harmony). Japan's island geography and historical social structure created extreme emphasis on group cohesion. Direct refusal disrupts 和. Korea shares Confucian values but has a more confrontational historical narrative (resistance to invasions, social movements) that normalized directness in certain contexts.

Section 3: 迷惑 — The Concept of Not Causing Trouble

迷惑 (nuisance/trouble/inconvenience) is one of the most important cultural concepts in Japan. While Korea has 폐 (trouble, as in 폐를 끼치다), the Japanese concept of 迷惑 operates at a much higher sensitivity level.

The 迷惑 Sensitivity Spectrum

SituationIn KoreaIn Japan
Talking on phone on train Generally acceptable Strongly avoided — マナーモード (manner mode) is expected
Eating while walking Common and acceptable Frowned upon except at festivals — creates crumbs, smells = 迷惑
Playing music from phone speaker Somewhat common in public Absolutely unacceptable — イヤホン (earphones) always
Strong perfume on train Generally tolerated Considered 迷惑 — imposing your scent on others
Being late by 5 minutes Often acceptable socially Requires apology: 遅れてすみません
Not finishing food at restaurant Sometimes acceptable Wastes the chef's effort = 迷惑 to the restaurant
Key Phrases for Avoiding 迷惑
  • ご迷惑をおかけして申し訳ございません — I sincerely apologize for the trouble (폐를 끼쳐서 죄송합니다)
  • お邪魔します — I'm intruding (said when entering someone's home) (실례합니다)
  • お手数ですが — I know this is troublesome, but... (번거로우시겠지만)
  • すみません、ちょっとよろしいですか — Excuse me, do you have a moment? (죄송합니다, 잠시 괜찮으세요?)

Section 4: Business Culture Differences

名刺交換 — Business Card Exchange

The Ritual (Not Just an Exchange)

  1. Present your card with both hands, text facing the recipient
  2. Receive their card with both hands, saying 頂戴いたします ("I humbly receive this")
  3. Study their card carefully — do NOT write on it, bend it, or put it in your back pocket
  4. Place it on the table during the meeting, positioned by where they sit
  5. After the meeting, store it respectfully in a card case (名刺入れ)

Korean business card exchange is similar but less ritualized. In Korea, you might receive a card with one hand from a peer. In Japan, always both hands, even with juniors.

Meeting Protocols

Korean Meetings (회의)

  • Can express disagreement during meeting
  • 제 생각은 좀 다른데요 (My thought is a bit different) is acceptable
  • Decisions can be made in the room
  • Follow-up by Kakao or email

Japanese Meetings (会議)

  • Decisions often pre-arranged (根回し)
  • Meeting is for formal confirmation, not debate
  • Disagreement expressed privately, before/after
  • Minutes (議事録) are critical
根回し (Nemawashi) — The Pre-Meeting

In Japan, the real meeting happens before the meeting. 根回し literally means "going around the roots" (of a tree before transplanting it). You informally discuss proposals with key stakeholders individually, building consensus before the formal meeting. The meeting itself is ceremonial. Koreans do this too (사전 조율), but Japanese 根回し is more systematic and expected.

飲み会 vs 회식 — Drinking Culture

Korean 회식

  • Often semi-mandatory, multiple rounds (1차, 2차, 3차)
  • Pouring for others is important
  • Turn away from seniors when drinking
  • Can get quite boisterous
  • Refusing alcohol increasingly accepted

Japanese 飲み会

  • Important for relationship building (飲みニケーション)
  • Pouring for others: always pour for seniors first
  • Wait for 乾杯 (toast) before drinking
  • More restrained than Korean 회식
  • 無礼講 (dropping formality) is declared but not fully real

Key vocabulary: お酌 (pouring for others), 二次会 (second round), 割り勘 (splitting the bill), おごる (treating someone)

Section 5: Regional Variation — 関西弁 and Beyond

Standard Japanese (標準語) is based on Tokyo speech. But Japan has rich regional dialects. The most important to know is 関西弁 (Kansai dialect), spoken in Osaka, Kyoto, and Kobe.

Tokyo and Osaka have distinct cultural personalities, much like Seoul and Busan. Kansai dialect is warmer, more direct, and more emotionally expressive.

Should You Learn 関西弁?

Not actively — learn to recognize it, not produce it. Using 関西弁 as a non-native speaker sounds odd (like a foreigner speaking in a Busan accent in Korea). However, understanding it is essential because you will encounter it in media, travel, and many Japanese friends from the Kansai region. Focus on recognition, not production.

Essential Cultural Vocabulary

義理 (Obligation)

Social debts that must be repaid. 義理チョコ (obligation chocolate on Valentine's Day) captures this perfectly. Similar to Korean 의리 but more formalized in Japan. Every favor creates a debt.

遠慮 (Restraint/Reserve)

Holding back to avoid imposing. When offered seconds, say 遠慮します at least once. The host will insist. You may then accept. This dance exists in Korea too (사양합니다) but is more ritualized in Japan.

もったいない (Wastefulness)

Deep aversion to waste — food, resources, potential. Not just environmental but spiritual: everything has value. Korean 아깝다 is close but もったいない carries stronger moral weight.

我慢 (Endurance)

Bearing pain or hardship silently. Valued as a virtue, not just a necessity. Korean 참다/인내 is similar, but Japanese 我慢 is elevated to a social expectation — complaining is itself 迷惑.

おもてなし (Hospitality)

Anticipating needs before they're expressed. Not just service but proactive care. Korean hospitality (정) is warmer and more emotional; Japanese おもてなし is more precise and anticipatory.

報連相 (Report-Connect-Consult)

報告・連絡・相談 = Report, inform, consult. The holy trinity of Japanese workplace communication. Not doing 報連相 is a career-ending mistake. Korean workplaces value this too but the term and system are distinctly Japanese.
