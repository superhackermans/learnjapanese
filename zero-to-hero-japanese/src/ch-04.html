Chapter 04Pronunciation — Japanese Phonetics for Korean Speakers (発音)

You already know hiragana and katakana. Now it's time to make them sound right. Good news: Japanese phonetics are simpler than Korean. Fewer consonants, simpler syllable structure, no 받침 (final consonants). You're downshifting. But simplicity brings its own traps — because your Korean habits will try to add complexity where Japanese doesn't want it.

What's EASIER Than Korean

Fewer consonants: Korean has a 3-way distinction (ㄱ/ㅋ/ㄲ). Japanese has only 2-way (voiced/voiceless). That's less to juggle.
Simpler syllables: Every Japanese syllable is (C)V — consonant + vowel, or just a vowel. No consonant clusters, no 받침.
No final consonants: Korean 학 (hak) has a ㄱ 받침. Japanese がく (gaku) always ends in a vowel. You never have to worry about unreleased stops.
You're downshifting. Think of it as going from a 6-speed manual to an automatic.

Consonant Mapping: Korean 3-Way vs. Japanese 2-Way

Korean stops come in three flavors: plain (ㄱ), aspirated (ㅋ), and tense (ㄲ). Japanese only has two: voiceless (か) and voiced (が). This is the single biggest source of Korean-accented Japanese.

Key Insight

When you see か, think ㄱ, not ㅋ. When you see た, think ㄷ, not ㅌ. Japanese voiceless stops are unaspirated — closer to Korean plain consonants than aspirated ones. Over-aspiration is the #1 Korean accent marker in Japanese.

The Six Pronunciation Traps

These are the specific sounds where Korean habits will lead you astray. Each trap is ranked by difficulty from one star (easy fix) to five stars (long-term project).

Trap 1: Over-Aspiration (Difficulty: ★★★)

Problem: Korean 3-way stops mapped to Japanese 2-way
Korean speakers reach for ㅋ/ㅌ/ㅍ when they should use ㄱ/ㄷ/ㅂ
What it sounds like when wrong: かわいい sounds like "KHAWAII" with a strong puff of air. Japanese listeners hear it as over-emphatic or foreign.

Fix: Hold a tissue in front of your mouth. Say か — the tissue should barely move. If it flutters, you're aspirating. Use your Korean ㄱ/ㄷ/ㅂ position, not ㅋ/ㅌ/ㅍ.

Mnemonic: "No K-pop puff!" — Japanese consonants are quiet and gentle. Save the ㅋ for Korean.

Practice: Say these pairs, keeping aspiration minimal:
かさ (kasa, umbrella) — not "khasa"
たべる (taberu, to eat) — not "thaberu"
ぱん (pan, bread) — not "phan"

Trap 2: Rounded う (Difficulty: ★★)

Problem: Korean 우 is rounded; Japanese う is unrounded
Your lips pucker for 우 but should stay relaxed for う
What it sounds like when wrong: すし sounds like "sooshi" with an English "oo" quality. It's immediately noticeable.

Fix: Say 우, then gradually relax your lips until they're almost flat. The tongue position stays the same, but the lips go neutral. Think of lazily saying "uh" but higher in the mouth.

Mnemonic: "Lazy lips!" — Japanese う is the laziest vowel. No effort to round.

Practice: Say these with relaxed lips:
つくる (tsukuru, to make)
ふゆ (fuyu, winter)
くつ (kutsu, shoes)

Trap 3: Long Vowels (Difficulty: ★★★★)

Problem: Vowel length changes meaning in Japanese
Korean doesn't distinguish vowel length (modern standard)
What it sounds like when wrong: Saying おばさん (aunt) when you mean おばあさん (grandmother). Or saying ビル (building) when you mean ビール (beer). Embarrassing at best, confusing at worst.

Fix: Count beats. Each kana = one beat (mora). おばさん = 4 beats. おばあさん = 5 beats. Tap your finger for each mora. The long vowel gets two taps.

Mnemonic: "Every kana gets its own drumbeat." Short vowel = one tap. Long vowel = two taps. Never rush.

Practice critical pairs:
おじさん (ojisan, uncle) vs. おじいさん (ojiisan, grandfather)
ここ (koko, here) vs. こうこう (koukou, high school)
ビル (biru, building) vs. ビール (biiru, beer)
え (e, picture) vs. ええ (ee, yes/agreement)

Trap 4: つ (tsu) (Difficulty: ★★)

Problem: The ts- onset doesn't exist in Korean
Korean speakers substitute 쓰, 추, or 스 — all wrong
What it sounds like when wrong: つき (moon) sounds like "chuki" or "ssuki." Native speakers will understand but notice.

Fix: Start from ㄷ position (tongue behind upper teeth). Now release into す, not 추. It's t + su fused into one onset. Practice: slowly say "t...su...t...su...tsu...tsu." Speed up until it's one smooth sound.

Mnemonic: "Tsunami starts with TSU!" — You already say this word correctly in English. Channel that same onset.

Practice:
つき (tsuki, moon) — not "chuki"
つくえ (tsukue, desk)
なつ (natsu, summer)
まつ (matsu, to wait / pine tree)

Trap 5: ふ (fu) (Difficulty: ★)

Problem: Bilabial fricative — not English "f" or Korean 후
Neither English "f" (teeth + lip) nor Korean 후 (glottal) is correct
What it sounds like when wrong: Using English "f" (lower lip touches upper teeth) sounds harsh. Using Korean 후 sounds too breathy. Both are noticeable.

Fix: Bring both lips close together (like you're about to whistle or blow out a candle) and push air through the gap. No teeth involved! It's between "f" and "h."

Mnemonic: "Blow out a birthday candle" — soft, both lips, no teeth. That puff is ふ.

Practice:
ふね (fune, ship)
ふゆ (fuyu, winter)
ふじさん (Fujisan, Mt. Fuji)
とうふ (toufu, tofu)

Trap 6: Pitch Accent (Difficulty: ★★★★★)

This is the boss-level trap. Korean is a stress-accent language (though it's subtle). Japanese is a pitch-accent language — meaning the melody of a word (high vs. low pitch on each mora) determines its identity. Wrong pitch doesn't usually cause misunderstanding, but it's the difference between sounding fluent and sounding foreign.

Problem: Pitch distinguishes otherwise identical words
Korean doesn't use pitch this way — it's an entirely new concept
What it sounds like when wrong: All your words sound "flat" or have wrong emphasis. Like speaking English in monotone — understandable but robotic.

Fix (short-term): Don't stress about pitch accent initially. Focus on the other 5 traps first. But start noticing pitch when you listen to Japanese. The most important rule: the pitch of the 2nd mora is always different from the 1st mora. If mora 1 is low, mora 2 is high, and vice versa.

Fix (long-term): Use pitch accent dictionaries (like the OJAD online tool). Learn the 4 patterns: heiban (flat), atamadaka (head-high), nakadaka (mid-drop), and odaka (tail-drop).

Mnemonic: "Japanese words are melodies, not drums." Korean emphasis is about strength; Japanese is about melody. Hum the tune, don't pound the beat.

Minimal pairs (same kana, different pitch = different word):
はし = chopsticks (HA-shi, high-low) vs. bridge (ha-SHI, low-high)
あめ = rain (A-me, high-low) vs. candy (a-ME, low-high)
かき = persimmon (KA-ki, high-low) vs. oyster (ka-KI, low-high)
Priority Check

Pitch accent is important for fluency but not critical for comprehension. Context resolves most ambiguity. Focus your energy on Traps 1-4 first (aspiration, う, long vowels, つ). Pitch accent is a lifelong refinement project.

Double Consonants — The Small っ

Small っ (tsu) represents a brief pause or "catch" before the next consonant. Think of it as a silent beat. Korean has a similar concept: 쌍자음 (double consonants) in words like 빨리. The small っ works differently (it's a held silence), but the concept of "something extra between syllables" should feel familiar.

っ = one beat of held silence before the next consonant
Similar feel to Korean 쌍자음, but realized as a pause
Mnemonic: "The tiny っ is a speed bump." You're driving along and suddenly — bump! — pause — then continue. The word gets one extra mora of silence.

Examples:
きて (kite, 2 beats) = "come!" vs. きって (kitte, 3 beats) = "stamp"
かこ (kako, 2 beats) = "past" vs. かっこ (kakko, 3 beats) = "parentheses/style"
いっぱい (ippai, 4 beats) = "full" vs. いぱい = not a word
