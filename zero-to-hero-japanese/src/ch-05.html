Chapter 05Survival Japanese -- Phrases You Can Use Today

Memory Technique Spotlight: Context-Dependent Memory

You remember things best in the context where you learned them (Godden & Baddeley, 1975). Each survival phrase is taught within a vivid scene — imagine yourself IN the restaurant, AT the station, INSIDE the shop. When you're actually there, the phrases will come flooding back.

Palace Assignment: The Street (집에서 역까지)

Build a memory palace along your route from home to the nearest station. Restaurant phrases at the restaurant you pass, shopping phrases at the convenience store, direction phrases at the intersection, transportation phrases at the station entrance.

You can now read hiragana, katakana, and pronounce Japanese correctly. This chapter puts those skills to work immediately. No grammar theory yet -- just learn complete phrases as functional units, with Korean parallels showing why they make structural sense.

"You don't need to understand WHY a sentence works to USE it. You say 'sumimasen' the same way you started saying '실례합니다' as a child -- you learn the whole phrase, use it, and the grammar understanding comes later. This chapter gives you 100+ phrases you can use TODAY."
How to Use This Chapter

Every phrase includes: Japanese (kana + kanji), Romaji, literal breakdown, Korean equivalent, a mnemonic, and a context note. Treat each phrase as a complete unit. Memorize the sound, use it, and grammar understanding will come in later chapters.

5A: Restaurant Japanese (レストラン)

The complete restaurant flow -- from walking in to paying the bill. This is the single most useful scenario for any traveler to Japan.

Entering the Restaurant

SituationJapaneseRomajiLiteral BreakdownKorean EquivalentMnemonic
Getting attention すみません sumimasen "not settled" (apology form) 저기요 / 실례합니다 "Sue me, Ma'am, SEN(d) help"
"Table for 2" 二人です futari desu two-people is 두 명이요 "Who's the TALL-ree? Two of us"
"Do you have a table?" 席はありますか？ seki wa arimasu ka? seat-[topic] exist-[polite]-[question] 자리 있어요? "SEEK-ee a seat -- is there one?"
"Non-smoking, please" 禁煙でお願いします kin'en de onegai shimasu no-smoking-[by] please 금연으로 부탁합니다 Korean 금연 = 禁煙 -- Cognate
Context Note

In Japan, you often don't need to flag down staff. Many restaurants have a call button (呼び出しボタン) at the table. At smaller restaurants, a polite すみません works perfectly. When entering, the staff will ask 何名様ですか？ (nanmeisama desu ka? = "How many guests?") -- just hold up fingers and say 二人です.

Ordering Food

Context Note: Restaurant Ordering

Most Japanese restaurants have picture menus or plastic food displays outside. Point at the item and say これをお願いします -- this works everywhere. At izakayas, saying おすすめは何ですか？ is a great conversation starter with the staff.

Paying the Bill

SituationJapaneseRomajiLiteral BreakdownKorean EquivalentMnemonic
"Check, please" お会計お願いします okaikei onegai shimasu [hon]-accounting please 계산해 주세요 "Oh-KAI-KAY = 회계 (accounting) -- Cognate"
"Can I pay by card?" カードで払えますか？ kaado de haraemasu ka? card-[by] can-pay-[question] 카드로 계산돼요? "CARD + HARA-eh = pay"
"Together / Separate" 一緒に / 別々に issho ni / betsubetsu ni together-[adv] / separate-separate-[adv] 같이 / 따로따로 "ISSHO = together. BETSU-BETSU = split-split"
"Thank you (after meal)" ごちそうさまでした gochisousama deshita "it was a feast" (set phrase) 잘 먹었습니다 "Go-CHEE-SO-SAH-MAH -- like bowing after a feast"
Elaborative Interrogation

WHY does Japanese have a dedicated phrase for "thank you for the meal" (ごちそうさまでした) while Korean also has 잘 먹었습니다?

Both cultures share the East Asian tradition of expressing gratitude for food. The Japanese phrase literally means "it was a feast (ごちそう)" while the Korean means "I ate well." Both signal respect to the cook/host. This is one of many cultural parallels where the concept transfers perfectly but the specific words differ.

5B: Shopping Japanese (買い物)

Whether at a convenience store, department store, or market -- these phrases cover every shopping situation.

"How much?" いくらですか？ ikura desu ka? how-much is-[question] 얼마예요? "EE-KOO-RAH -- sounds like 'it'll CURE ya' to know the price"
"I'm just looking" 見ているだけです mite iru dake desu seeing-[progressive] only is 그냥 구경하고 있어요 "ME-tay (seeing) + DAKE (only)"

"Can I try it on?" 試着できますか？ shichaku dekimasu ka? try-on can-do-[question] 시착할 수 있어요? 시착/試着 -- Cognate Exact same word!
"Too expensive" ちょっと高いですね chotto takai desu ne a-little expensive is-[agreement] 좀 비싸네요 "CHOTTO = 좀 (a little). TAKAI = expensive"

Context Note: Shopping in Japan

At convenience stores (コンビニ), the clerk may ask 袋はいりますか？ (fukuro wa irimasu ka? = "Do you need a bag?"). Bags cost a few yen. Say はい、お願いします (yes, please) or いいえ、大丈夫です (no, I'm fine). Tax-free shopping is available at many stores for tourists spending over 5,000 yen -- just show your passport!

5D: Transportation (交通)

Japan's public transportation is world-class. These phrases will get you on the right train, to the right platform, and off at the right stop.

"One ticket to [X]" [X]まで一枚 [X] made ichimai [X]-until one-flat-thing [X]까지 한 장 "MADE = 까지 (until). ICHI-MAI = one flat thing"
"Which platform?" 何番ホームですか？ nanban hoomu desu ka? what-number platform is-[question] 몇 번 홈이에요? "NAN-BAN = what number. HOOMU = home/platform"

"Last train" 終電 shuuden end-electric (= last electric train) 막차 "SHUU-DEN = 종전(終電) -- Cognate-ish!"

Context Note: Trains in Japan

The last train (終電) is critically important -- it's usually around midnight, and missing it means an expensive taxi ride or waiting until 5 AM. Always check the schedule! Use IC cards (Suica/Pasmo) to avoid buying tickets. The phrase 乗り換え (norikae = transfer) appears constantly on station signs.

5E: Essential Polite Phrases (基本フレーズ)

These are the foundation of daily Japanese interaction. Master these first -- they cover greetings, thanks, apologies, and basic communication.

Greetings

SituationJapaneseRomajiLiteral BreakdownKorean EquivalentMnemonic
Good morning おはようございます ohayou gozaimasu "it is early" (polite) 안녕하세요 (아침) "Oh-HI-YO -- 'Oh hi yo!' in the morning"
Hello (daytime) こんにちは konnichiwa "this day" (today greeting) 안녕하세요 (낮) "KON-NICHI-WA -- 'today' greeting"
Good evening こんばんは konbanwa "this evening" 안녕하세요 (저녁) "KON-BAN-WA -- 'this evening' greeting"
Goodnight おやすみなさい oyasumi nasai "please rest" 안녕히 주무세요 "Oh-YAH-SOO-ME -- 'oh yes, ZOOM into sleep'"
Goodbye (formal) さようなら sayounara "if it must be so" (farewell) 안녕히 가세요 "SAY-YOU-NAH-RAH -- formal. Use じゃあね for casual"
Goodbye (casual) じゃあね jaa ne "well then" 잘 가 / 바이 "JAH-NEH -- see ya!"
Elaborative Interrogation

WHY does Korean use the same greeting (안녕하세요) for all times of day, while Japanese has different greetings for morning, afternoon, and evening?

Korean 안녕하세요 literally asks "are you at peace?" -- a universal concept. Japanese greetings are time-stamped abbreviations of longer classical phrases: おはよう from "it is early," こんにちは from "how is today?", こんばんは from "how is this evening?" Both systems work -- Korean is simpler to learn, Japanese requires memorizing three forms but tells you the time of day!

The すみません Multi-Tool

すみません is the Swiss Army knife of Japanese. It means "excuse me," "sorry," "thank you" (for trouble caused), and "hey, could you help?" all at once. Just like Korean 저기요 + 죄송합니다 combined. When in doubt, すみません is almost never wrong.

Emergency Numbers in Japan

110 = Police (警察 keisatsu, Korean 경찰 -- Cognate)
119 = Fire and Ambulance (消防 shoubou / 救急 kyuukyuu)
The nearest police box (交番 kouban) can help with lost items, directions, and emergencies.

Notice the pattern: さん/삼, し/사, しち/칠, く/구, せん/천 -- these all come from the same Chinese source. The sound shifts follow predictable rules you'll learn in Chapter 11. For now, use these similarities as memory anchors!
