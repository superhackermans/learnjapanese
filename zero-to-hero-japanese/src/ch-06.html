Chapter 06Counters -- The Shape of Counting (助数詞)

Memory Technique Spotlight: Chunking + Phonetic Association

Japanese counters seem overwhelming -- dozens of counters, each with irregular sound changes at specific numbers. The key is chunking by pattern. All h-row counters (本, 匹, 杯) follow the EXACT same sound-change rules. Learn the pattern once, apply it to all three. Then create phonetic association chains: "ippon" sounds like "I PAWN one bone" -- vivid mental images lock each reading into long-term memory faster than rote repetition.

Research shows chunking reduces cognitive load by ~80% (Miller, 1956). Instead of memorizing 120+ individual readings, you learn 3 patterns and apply them systematically.

Palace Assignment: The Kitchen (부엌)

Your kitchen becomes the home of all counters. Assign each counter to a specific kitchen location:

  • つ on the counter (countertop = counter!) -- your universal safety net sits right where you work
  • 個 in the egg carton -- eggs are small, round objects you count with 個
  • 人 at the dinner table -- people sit here, count seats for guests
  • 匹 near the pet food bowl -- animals come here to eat
  • 本 by the knife block -- knives are long, thin objects standing upright
  • 杯 at the coffee machine -- cups and glasses of liquid brewed here
  • 枚 on the cutting board -- flat sliced things laid out flat
  • 台 by the microwave -- machines and appliances counted here
  • 冊 on the cookbook shelf -- books and volumes stored here
  • 足 at the kitchen doormat -- shoes and slippers left at the entrance
  • 階 on the step stool -- climbing floors, going up levels
  • 回 on the kitchen timer -- counting times and repetitions

In Chapter 05, you learned to say 二人です (table for two) and ビール二杯お願いします (two beers, please). You were already using counters. This chapter gives you the complete system -- every major counter, every sound change, every conjugation from 1 to 10.

"Korean speakers already use counters every day. You never say '사과 셋' -- you say '사과 세 개.' Japanese works exactly the same way. This chapter isn't learning a new concept -- it's translating a system you already own into a new language."

Introduction -- Why Counters Exist

In English, you can say "three cats" or "five books" -- just number + noun. But in Japanese (and Korean, and Chinese), you need a counter word (助数詞, 조수사) between the number and the thing. The counter classifies the shape, size, or type of what you're counting.

Think of it this way: English actually has counters too, just fewer of them. You say "two sheets of paper," "three head of cattle," "a pair of shoes." Japanese (and Korean) simply have more of these classifiers and use them consistently.

Your Korean Advantage

Korean has an almost identical counter system. You already know:

  • 개 -- general objects (사과 세 개)
  • 명 -- people (학생 다섯 명)
  • 마리 -- animals (고양이 두 마리)
  • 잔 -- cups/glasses (커피 한 잔)
  • 병 -- bottles (맥주 세 병)
  • 장 -- flat things (종이 열 장)
  • 권 -- books (책 두 권)
  • 대 -- machines/vehicles (차 한 대)

The SYSTEM is identical. You just need to learn which Japanese counter maps to which Korean counter. Many are even cognates -- same Chinese character, similar pronunciation.

Korean System

사과 + 세 + 개

apple + three + [counter]

Japanese System

りんご + 三つ

apple + three-[counter]

The Big Difference: Sound Changes

Korean counters attach cleanly: 한 개, 두 개, 세 개 -- the counter 개 never changes form. In Japanese, many counters change pronunciation depending on which number precedes them. For example, 本 (hon) becomes ippon at 1, sanbon at 3, and roppon at 6. This is the main challenge -- and this chapter gives you every single change, charted and patterned.

The Native つ System -- Your Universal Safety Net

The native Japanese counting system (ひとつ、ふたつ、みっつ...) works for almost anything up to 10. It's like using 개 for everything in Korean -- not always elegant, but always understood. Learn this first as your safety net.

When to Use つ

Use つ when you don't know the specific counter, in casual speech, or for general objects. At stores, 三つください (mittsu kudasai, "three, please") is perfectly natural. Staff will understand even if there's a more specific counter.

Important: つ Only Works 1-10

The native つ system only goes up to 10. For numbers above 10, you MUST use a specific counter (個, 本, 枚, etc.) with Sino-Japanese numbers. At 11+, there is no "safety net" -- you need to know the right counter.

Korean Parallel: Native Numbers for 1-2

Notice how both languages use native number readings for 1 and 2 people: Korean uses 한/두 (native) rather than 일/이 (Sino), and Japanese uses hitori/futari (native) rather than *ichinin/*ninin. The pattern is the same -- irregular at the small numbers, regular from 3 up.

Small Objects 個 (ko) -- Complete Chart

The 個 counter is for small, discrete, often roundish objects -- eggs, apples, balls, boxes. Korean cognate: 個 = 개. Cognate Same kanji, same concept!

Sound Change Pattern: k-doubling

When the preceding number ends in a clipped consonant (1, 6, 8, 10), the k doubles: ko becomes kko. Think of it as the consonant "bumping" into the k and doubling it.

Long & Thin Objects 本 (hon) -- Complete Chart

The 本 counter is for anything long and cylindrical: bottles, pens, umbrellas, trees, roads, bananas, films, telephone calls. Think "long like a BONE." Korean parallels: 병 (bottles), 자루 (pens).

The h-Row Sound Change Pattern

This is the MOST IMPORTANT sound change pattern in Japanese counters. When h meets certain numbers:

  • h → p at 1, 6, 8, 10 (doubles: ippon, roppon, happon, juppon)
  • h → b at 3 and ? (sanbOn, nanbOn)

This SAME pattern applies to ALL h-row counters: 本, 匹, 杯. Learn it once, use it three times.

Animals -- Small 匹 (hiki) + Large 頭 (tou)

Korean uses 마리 for ALL animals. Japanese distinguishes by size: 匹 (hiki) for small/medium animals (cats, dogs, fish, insects) and 頭 (tou) for large animals (horses, cows, elephants, whales).

Which Animals Use Which Counter?

匹: cats, dogs, fish, insects, mice, rabbits, birds (small/medium)
頭: horses, cows, elephants, whales, gorillas, bears (large)
The dividing line is roughly "can you carry it?" If yes → 匹. If no → 頭.

はたち (hatachi) -- The Big Exception

Twenty years old is always はたち (hatachi), NOT *nijussai. This is because 20 is the traditional age of adulthood in Japan (成人, seijin). Even though the legal age changed to 18 in 2022, はたち persists in the language. Korean parallel: how 스무 살 uses the native number rather than 이십 세.

No Sound Changes (Easy Counters!)

Some counters have NO sound changes at all: 枚 (mai), 台 (dai), 人 (nin, from 3+). The initial consonant (m, d, n) doesn't trigger doubling or voicing. Enjoy the free pass!

Korean Parallel: Consonant Assimilation

Korean has similar consonant assimilation rules. Think of 합니다 → 합니까, or how 독립 is pronounced [dongnim]. The underlying phonetic principle is the same: when two consonants collide, one assimilates to the other. Japanese counters just have this happen more visibly in the spelling.

Polite Age Question

Asking someone's age directly with 何歳ですか can be too direct. The polite form is おいくつですか (oikutsu desu ka) -- using the generic counter with the polite お prefix. Korean equivalent: 연세가 어떻게 되세요?

Word Order -- Where Counters Go in a Sentence

Japanese counters can go in two positions. Both are correct, but they create slightly different nuances.

Pattern 1: Noun + Number-Counter

りんご三個

ringo sanko

More natural in speech. The counter floats after the noun, often before the verb.

Korean parallel: 사과 세 개

Pattern 2: Number-Counter + の + Noun

三個のりんご

sanko no ringo

More literary/formal. The counter modifies the noun directly with の.

Korean parallel: 세 개의 사과

Full Sentence Examples

PatternJapaneseReadingKorean
Pattern 1 りんごを三個ください ringo wo sanko kudasai 사과 세 개 주세요
Pattern 2 三個のりんごをください sanko no ringo wo kudasai 세 개의 사과를 주세요
Pattern 1 猫を二匹飼っています neko wo nihiki katte imasu 고양이 두 마리 키우고 있어요
Pattern 1 ビールを三杯飲んだ biiru wo sanbai nonda 맥주 세 잔 마셨어
Tip: Just Use Pattern 1

In everyday speech, Pattern 1 (noun + counter) is far more common. It matches the Korean word order you already know: 사과 세 개 = りんご三個. Use Pattern 2 only in formal writing or when you want to emphasize the quantity.

  • 階 (kai) = 계/층 -- floors Cognate
  • 杯 (hai) = 배/잔 -- cups, glasses Cognate
  • 冊 (satsu) = 책 -- books (same radical 冊) Cognate
  • 歳 (sai) = 세 -- age Cognate

That's 7 out of 16 counters where the kanji alone tells you what it counts. Korean gives you a massive head start.

Pro Tip: Counter + ください is Your Power Phrase

The formula [number][counter] + ください handles most shopping and ordering situations. Just swap in the right counter:

  • 三つください -- "Three (of those), please" (safest)
  • 二杯ください -- "Two cups/glasses, please"
  • 一枚ください -- "One (flat thing/ticket), please"
  • 五個ください -- "Five (small items), please"
