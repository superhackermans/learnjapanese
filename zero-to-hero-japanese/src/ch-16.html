Chapter 16Writing — From Casual Messages to Business Emails

Memory Technique Spotlight: Production Effect + Error-Based Learning

Writing (producing) information creates much stronger memory traces than reading (receiving) it. Every writing exercise forces your brain to actively retrieve and construct — and even your mistakes become powerful learning signals through error-based correction.

Reading and writing are two sides of the same coin, but writing demands active production. This chapter covers the full spectrum: casual LINE messages, semi-formal blog posts, and formal business emails. As a Korean speaker, you already understand register-switching (반말/존댓말) — Japanese writing follows the same principle with different tools.

"In Korean, the difference between texting a friend and emailing a boss is enormous. Japanese is exactly the same. Master the register, and the words follow."

Section 1: Casual Writing — LINE, Twitter/X, and Texting

Casual Japanese writing drops formality aggressively — omitting particles, contracting verb forms, and relying on emoji and kaomoji. If you text in Korean, the patterns are remarkably parallel.

LINE conversation between friends

Japanese

A: 今日ひま？

Korean Equivalent

A: 오늘 시간 있어?
B: 응, 왜?
A: 시부야에서 영화 안 볼래?

Section 2: Semi-Formal Writing — Blog Posts and Reviews

Blog posts and product reviews sit between casual and formal. They use polite endings (です/ます) but maintain a personal, approachable tone. This is the 해요체 equivalent in Korean.

Blog post / review style

Japanese Blog Post

先日、京都に行ってきました。紅葉がとてもきれいで、写真をたくさん撮りました。特に清水寺からの景色は最高でした。京都に行く方は、ぜひ秋に行くことをおすすめします。

Korean Blog Post

며칠 전에 교토에 다녀왔어요. 단풍이 너무 예뻐서 사진을 많이 찍었어요. 특히 기요미즈데라에서의 경치는 최고였어요. 교토에 가시는 분은 꼭 가을에 가시는 것을 추천해요.

Section 3: Formal Writing — Business Email Structure

Japanese business email (ビジネスメール) follows a rigid structure. As a Korean speaker, you already know that business correspondence demands a specific frame (비즈니스 메일의 틀). Japanese is remarkably similar in structure but has its own set phrases that must be memorized.

Email Structure Map

Both Japanese and Korean business emails follow the exact same structure:

1. Subject line → 2. Recipient → 3. Greeting formula → 4. Self-identification → 5. Purpose of email → 6. Details/request → 7. Apology for the trouble → 8. Closing formula → 9. Signature block. The order is identical. Only the specific phrases differ.

Section 4: Common Writing Mistakes Korean Speakers Make

Your Korean is a huge advantage, but it also creates specific interference patterns. These are the most common writing mistakes Korean speakers make in Japanese.

Mistake Direct Translation of Korean Particles

✗ 友達に会いに行く (going to meet a friend)

This one is actually correct! But watch out for: 친구한테 → ✗ 友達に電話する vs ✓ 友達に電話する. In this case it works, but -에게 doesn't always map to に. Context matters.

Mistake Overusing は Where が is Needed

✗ 誰は来ましたか？

✓ 誰が来ましたか？

Korean 누가 uses the subject marker 가, and Japanese question words similarly require が, not は. But Korean speakers sometimes default to は because 은/는 is more frequently used.

Mistake Wrong Kanji from Hanja Assumption

Korean 인기 (人氣) → Japanese 人気 (にんき) works perfectly. But 공부 (工夫) → Japanese 工夫 means "ingenuity/device," NOT "study." Study = 勉強. These false friends (偽友) trip up Korean speakers frequently.

Mistake Using Korean Word Order for Modifiers

✗ 赤いの大きい車 (red big car)

✓ 大きい赤い車 / 赤くて大きい車

Japanese has subtle preferences for modifier order. When stacking adjectives, connect them with て-form (赤くて大きい車) or follow Japanese conventions.

Mistake Mixing Registers in One Text

✗ お世話になっております。明日の件だけど、確認いたしました。

✓ お世話になっております。明日の件について、確認いたしました。

だけど is casual; について is formal. Never mix casual connectors into a formal email. Same as using 근데 in a Korean business email instead of 그런데 or ~에 대해.

Mistake Forgetting Sentence-Final Differences

Korean casual sentences can end in -야/-이야 for copula. Japanese uses だ/だよ. But Korean -거든 (providing a reason casually) doesn't map to any single Japanese ending. Don't force Korean sentence-endings onto Japanese — learn the Japanese endings independently.

Elaborative Interrogation

WHY are hanja-kanji false friends (like 工夫) particularly dangerous for Korean speakers?

Because your brain automatically activates the Korean meaning when you see familiar characters. This is "negative transfer" — when prior knowledge actively interferes with new learning. The solution is to maintain a personal "false friends" list and review it regularly. Key pairs to watch: 공부(工夫) ≠ 工夫(ingenuity), 대장(大將) ≠ 大丈夫(fine/OK), 매우(每) ≠ 毎(every — same character, but usage differs).
