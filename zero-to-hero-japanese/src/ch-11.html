The Korean Speaker's Triple Advantage

When learning kanji, Korean speakers have an advantage on three distinct layers. English speakers must learn all three from scratch.

Layer 1: Conceptual Understanding Instant Transfer

学 = 학

You already know this character means "to learn." English speakers must learn the meaning from scratch.

学生 = 학생 (학 + 생 = learn + life/birth)

Layer 2: Meaning Guessing High Hit Rate

経済学 = ?

You can guess the meaning using your Sino-Korean vocabulary: 경 (pass through) + 제 (cross/manage) + 학 (study) = economics. Even words you see for the first time become transparent when you break down the kanji.

Layer 3: On'yomi Prediction Regular Correspondence

학 → がく

Korean readings and Japanese on'yomi share regular phonological correspondences. Once you learn the patterns, you can predict the reading as well.

English speaker vs. Korean speaker -- the starting line for kanji study is entirely different.
Elaborative Interrogation

Why can we say Korean speakers "already know" kanji?

Roughly 60% of Korean vocabulary is Sino-Korean. Words like 학교 (學校, school), 경제 (經濟, economy), and 도서관 (圖書館, library) -- you may not write the hanja characters themselves, but you know that each syllable maps 1:1 to a specific hanja. This knowledge becomes an enormous lever when studying Japanese kanji.

Elaborative Interrogation

Why does Korean ㅂ become h in Japanese?

The original Chinese b/p-series consonants were preserved as ㅂ in Korean, but in Japanese they underwent a historical sound shift: p → ɸ → h. That is why 발 (發, "emit/depart") becomes はつ (hatsu) in Japanese. Interestingly, when rendaku (連濁, sequential voicing) occurs in Japanese compounds, the h can revert back to b (e.g., 見本 みほん → 手本 てほん, but お手本 おてほん).

Four-Step Kanji Learning Strategy

Step 1: Cognates First

Start by connecting Sino-Korean words you already know with their Japanese readings. Since you don't need to learn the meanings, you can focus solely on the readings.

E.g.: 학생 → 学生（がくせい）

Step 2: Use Sound Correspondences

Apply the six sound correspondence rules above to predict the on'yomi of new kanji. Your hit rate will steadily increase over time.

E.g.: 행 (ㅎ) → k → こう

Step 3: Learn Kun'yomi in Context

On'yomi (おんよみ) can be predicted via rules, but kun'yomi (くんよみ) should be learned naturally in context. Kun'yomi are linked to native Japanese words, so Sino-Korean knowledge does not help here.

E.g.: 食 -- on'yomi: しょく (식) / kun'yomi: た（べる）

Step 4: Decompose with Radicals

When you encounter an unfamiliar kanji, break it down into radicals. Identify the meaning radical + sound radical to simultaneously guess meaning and reading.

E.g.: 清 = 氵 (water) + 青 (청, blue/clear) → clear/pure

Elaborative Interrogation

Why do Japanese kanji have two types of readings -- on'yomi and kun'yomi?

When Korean adopted Chinese characters, it only borrowed the sound (on'yomi). When Japanese adopted Chinese characters, it borrowed the Chinese pronunciation (on'yomi) as well, but simultaneously attached the native Japanese word with the same meaning to the character. So 山 has on'yomi さん (산) while also having kun'yomi やま (native Japanese word for "mountain"). Korean does not have this dual system, which means kun'yomi is a genuinely new learning area for Korean speakers.
