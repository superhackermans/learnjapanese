Chapter 10Sentence Patterns -- Thinking in Japanese

Memory Technique Spotlight: Chunking + Schema Construction

Sentence patterns are "chunks" — pre-built units that your brain can deploy instantly without assembling from scratch. Master the chunk, and you can swap vocabulary in and out effortlessly. This is how native speakers achieve fluency — not by constructing each sentence from grammar rules.

You have been using these patterns since Chapter 05, building survival phrases and daily conversations. Now it is time to formalize them -- to understand the structural machinery so you can generate new sentences on your own, not just recall memorized ones.

"Patterns are the grammar you already feel. This chapter turns intuition into engineering."
Last Chapter Before Romaji Drops

Starting in Chapter 11, romaji disappears. From here on, you read Japanese in Japanese. This chapter still provides romaji as training wheels, but treat every example as a chance to practice reading hiragana and katakana directly. If you catch yourself reading the romaji first, force your eyes to the kana.

Existence -- いる / ある

Korean has one existence verb: 있다. It handles everything -- people, objects, ideas. Japanese splits this into two verbs based on whether the subject is animate or inanimate.

いる
iru
Animate (living, moving)
vs.
ある
aru
Inanimate (objects, concepts)

Korean 있다 covers both. Japanese forces you to choose.

Mnemonic

いる = eel -- an eel is ALIVE, it wriggles. Use いる for living things.
ある = R for Rocks -- rocks just sit there, dead. Use ある for inanimate things.

Core Examples

猫がいるneko ga iru고양이가 있다There is a catいる (alive)
本があるhon ga aru책이 있다There is a bookある (object)
子どもがいるkodomo ga iru아이가 있다There is a child / I have a childいる (alive)

犬が三匹いるinu ga sanbiki iru개가 세 마리 있다There are three dogsいる (alive)
椅子があるisu ga aru의자가 있다There is a chairある (object)

Tricky Cases -- The Edge of "Alive"

The animate/inanimate line is not always obvious. Here are the cases that trip up every learner:

Robots ある

ロボットがある (robotto ga aru)

Robots are machines, not alive. Even humanoid ones get ある.

Plants ある

花がある (hana ga aru)

Plants are alive biologically, but they do not move on their own. Japanese treats them as inanimate.

Ghosts いる

幽霊がいる！ (yuurei ga iru!)

Ghosts move, they have will. Even though they are dead, they behave as animate. Yes, really.

Fish in a Tank いる

魚がいる (sakana ga iru)

Living fish = いる. But fish on a plate (sashimi)? That is food: 刺身がある.

Taxis (waiting) ある

タクシーがある (takushii ga aru)

A taxi is a vehicle (object). But the driver inside? 運転手がいる (untenshu ga iru).

Dolls / Stuffed Animals ある

人形がある (ningyou ga aru)

Not alive. But a child playing pretend might say いる -- context can shift it.

Elaborative Interrogation

WHY does Korean use a single verb 있다 for existence while Japanese splits it into two?

Korean grammatically categorizes existence by formality level (있다/있습니다/있어요), not by animacy. Japanese grammatically categorizes existence by the nature of the subject itself. Both languages encode important distinctions -- they just choose different axes. Think of it as Korean caring about "how politely you say it" and Japanese caring about "what kind of thing you are talking about."

The animate/inanimate split -- Korean 있다 maps to both いる and ある.

Desire -- -たい

Korean expresses desire with -고 싶다, attached after the verb stem. Japanese uses -たい, also attached to the verb stem. The structure maps almost perfectly.

Korean

먹고 싶다

meokgo sipda

eat + want = want to eat

Japanese

食べたい

tabetai

eat (stem) + want = want to eat

Same logic. Take the verb stem, attach the desire suffix. Korean: stem + 고 싶다. Japanese: stem + たい.

Mnemonic

たい = tie -- you are TIED to your desire. You cannot let go. You are bound to wanting it.

Conjugation: Verb Stem + たい

Take the masu-stem (the form before ます) and add たい. The result conjugates like an い-adjective, not a verb.

-たい Conjugates Like an い-Adjective

This is the key difference from Korean. In Korean, -고 싶다 is a verb construction. In Japanese, -たい behaves like an adjective -- it has its own negative, past, and polite forms:

FormJapaneseRomajiKoreanMeaning
Present食べたいtabetai먹고 싶다want to eat
Negative食べたくないtabetakunai먹고 싶지 않다don't want to eat
Past食べたかったtabetakatta먹고 싶었다wanted to eat
Polite食べたいですtabetai desu먹고 싶어요want to eat (polite)
Past Negative食べたくなかったtabetakunakatta먹고 싶지 않았다didn't want to eat
Particle Note: を or が with -たい

With -たい, the object can take either を or が:
水を飲みたい / 水が飲みたい -- both mean "I want to drink water."
Using が emphasizes the desire itself (more emotional). Using を is neutral. Both are correct.

Giving and Receiving -- あげる / もらう / くれる

WARNING: Zero Korean Advantage Here

This is the ONE grammar point where Korean gives you zero advantage. Korean has 주다 (give) and 받다 (receive) -- two verbs, direction is clear from context. Japanese has three verbs, and the choice depends on the direction of the action relative to the speaker. This is genuinely hard. Take your time.

The Three Verbs

あげる (ageru)

Give AWAY from speaker

I give to you. He gives to her. Anyone gives to anyone other than the speaker.

~주다 (when I am the giver)

くれる (kureru)

Give TOWARD the speaker

Someone gives to me. Someone gives to my family/group. The gift flows toward the speaker.

~주다 (when someone gives to ME)

もらう (morau)

Receive

I receive from someone. The subject is the receiver.

~받다

Mnemonic: The River

Imagine a river. The speaker stands on one bank.
あげる = AWAY -- you throw the gift across the river, away from you.
くれる = COMES to you -- the gift floats down the current toward you.
もらう = BUCKET -- you dip a bucket into the river and scoop the gift up to yourself.

The river model -- direction is always relative to the speaker, not the subject.

Elaborative Interrogation

WHY does Japanese need three verbs when Korean manages with just two (주다/받다)?

Japanese encodes the speaker's perspective directly into the verb. くれる inherently carries gratitude -- it says "someone did me the favor of giving." Korean expresses this through tone, context, or explicit phrases like ~해 주다. Japanese builds the social relationship into the grammar itself. This is one reason Japanese is described as a "speaker-centric" language.

Relative Clauses -- Free Transfer

"Free." -- This pattern transfers 100% from Korean. Same structure, just different words.

In both Korean and Japanese, relative clauses go before the noun they modify. There is no "who," "which," or "that" -- the clause simply sits in front of the noun. English speakers struggle here. You will not.

Korean

[어제 내가 읽은] 책

[eoje naega ilgeun] chaek

[yesterday I read] book

Japanese

[昨日私が読んだ] 本

[kinou watashi ga yonda] hon

[yesterday I read] book

Identical structure. [Modifying clause] + Noun. No relative pronoun needed. 100% transfer

the movie I watched[내가 본] 영화[私が見た] 映画[watashi ga mita] eiga
the cake that Mom made[엄마가 만든] 케이크[お母さんが作った] ケーキ[okaasan ga tsukutta] keeki
the coffee that is on the table[테이블 위에 있는] 커피[テーブルの上にある] コーヒー[teeburu no ue ni aru] koohii
the place where we met[우리가 만난] 곳[私たちが会った] 場所[watashitachi ga atta] basho
the student who studies Japanese[일본어를 공부하는] 학생[日本語を勉強している] 学生[nihongo wo benkyou shiteiru] gakusei
The Rule

Whatever form the verb takes at the end of a sentence, that same form goes directly in front of the noun. Past tense? Use past form. Negative? Use negative form. Present progressive? Use ている form. The clause just "stacks" in front of the noun, exactly as in Korean.

Stacking Relative Clauses

Both languages can stack multiple modifiers. Watch:
[어제 엄마가 만든 맛있는] 케이크
[昨日お母さんが作ったおいしい] ケーキ
= "the delicious cake that Mom made yesterday"
Same ordering logic. The complexity grows identically.

Reported Speech -- と

Korean uses -라고 (and variants like -다고, -냐고) to mark quoted or reported speech. Japanese uses と. The structure is the same:

Korean

"간다"고 말했다

"ganda" go malhaessda

[quote] + quotation particle + said

Japanese

"行く"と言った

"iku" to itta

[quote] + quotation particle + said

Same structure. [Quote] + particle + verb of saying. Korean 라고 = Japanese と. 100% transfer

Elaborative Interrogation

WHY do both Korean and Japanese use a quotation particle while English does not?

English relies on word order and complementizers ("that," "whether") to mark reported speech. Korean and Japanese, as SOV agglutinative languages, use a particle to explicitly mark where the quote ends and the reporting verb begins. This is structurally necessary because in SOV languages, the verb comes last -- without a marker, you would not know where the quoted content ends. The particle acts as a "closing quotation bracket."
