Chapter 12Sino-Vocabulary Turbocharger — 漢字語 Power-Up

Memory Technique Spotlight: Sound Correspondence Rules + Generation Effect

Instead of memorizing thousands of vocabulary words, you'll learn ~10 sound correspondence rules that let you PREDICT Japanese readings from Korean. The Generation Effect means that predicting before seeing the answer creates significantly stronger memories than passive reading.

Korean and Japanese share 40-60% of their vocabulary through words derived from Chinese characters. This chapter systematically leverages that shared vocabulary to explosively expand your Japanese word power. Your knowledge of Korean Sino-vocabulary is your greatest weapon.

"If you know Korean Sino-vocabulary, half of Japanese vocabulary is already yours. Learn the sound correspondence rules, and you can predict the other half too."

Expanded Sound Correspondence Rules — Predicting Japanese Readings from Korean

In Chapter 11, you learned the basic sound correspondences. Now we expand each rule with more examples. Mastering these rules lets you predict Japanese on'yomi from Korean Sino-readings.

Core Principle

Chinese characters were transmitted separately to Korea and Japan from China. Even the same character developed different pronunciations depending on the era and route of transmission, but these changes follow regular patterns. Know the patterns, and you can predict the readings.

Elaborative Interrogation

Why does Korean ㅂ correspond to the Japanese は-row (h)?

Historically, the Japanese は-row underwent a sound shift from p → ɸ → h. When Chinese characters were first transmitted, the Japanese は-row was still pronounced [p], so the same sound as Korean ㅂ [p/b] was used. Over time, only the Japanese side shifted to h, while Korean preserved the b/p sound.

Correspondence map between Korean initial consonants and Japanese kana rows. Both ㅎ and ㄱ map to the か-row. ㅈ straddles both the さ-row and た-row.

False Friends (偽りの友) — Words That Look the Same but Mean Different Things

The most dangerous trap in Sino-cognates is words that use the same kanji but have different meanings in Korean and Japanese. Make sure to memorize the 30+ "false friends" below.

Warning: False friends cause communication accidents

Assuming the same kanji means the same thing can lead to serious misunderstandings. The higher the risk level, the more carefully you should study these words.

Elaborative Interrogation

Why do the same kanji have different meanings in Korean and Japanese?

After Chinese characters were transmitted, each country underwent its own independent semantic shifts. For example, 勉強 originally meant "to do something reluctantly" in Chinese. In Japan, the meaning expanded to "something you do even reluctantly = studying," while Korean preserved the original sense of "barely, with difficulty."

Compound Word Building — Inferring Meaning from Kanji Components

If you know the meaning of individual kanji, you can infer the meaning of unfamiliar compound words. This is the true power of kanji learning.

Core Principle: Kanji = LEGO Bricks

Most kanji words are built from combinations of 2-3 kanji. If you know each kanji's meaning, you can assemble the overall meaning.

Component Analysis Example 1

図書館

図(diagram) + 書(writing) + 館(hall)
= a hall for diagrams and writings = library

Component Analysis Example 2

自動販売機

自(self) + 動(move) + 販(sell) + 売(sell) + 機(machine)
= a machine that sells by itself = vending machine

Component Analysis Example 3

不動産

不(not) + 動(move) + 産(property)
= property that doesn't move = real estate

Component Analysis Example 4

心理学

心(mind) + 理(logic/principle) + 学(study)
= study of the mind's principles = psychology

Practical Tip: When You Encounter an Unknown Word

When you come across an unfamiliar Sino-Japanese word in text, try reading each kanji using its Korean Sino-reading. If a Korean word comes to mind, you'll instantly know the meaning. Example: 国際連合 → read as Korean: 국제연합 → that's the UN!

Knowing the kanji components lets you infer meaning from Korean knowledge and predict Japanese readings using sound correspondence rules.
