Chapter 09Daily Conversations -- Phrases That Get You Through the Day

Memory Technique Spotlight: Episode Encoding + Social Simulation

Conversations are stored as episodes — mini-movies in your memory. By mentally simulating social interactions (self-introductions, small talk, goodbyes), you create vivid episodic memories that activate automatically in real situations. The Self-Reference Effect means using YOUR actual name and details makes the encoding even stronger.

Chapter 05 gave you survival fragments -- enough to order food and ask for directions. Now, with particles (Ch07) and verb conjugation (Ch08) under your belt, it is time to speak in complete, connected sentences. This chapter teaches you the conversational patterns that turn a tourist into someone who can actually interact with people.

"Survival phrases get you through a transaction. Conversational patterns get you through a relationship."

8A: Self-Introduction (自己紹介 / 자기소개)

The self-introduction is a ritual in Japan. At a party, at work, at a language exchange -- you will deliver this script dozens of times. The structure is nearly identical to Korean, so the transfer is massive.

자기소개/自己紹介 -- Another Cognate!

자기소개 (自己紹介) and じこしょうかい (自己紹介) share the exact same kanji. Korean: ja-gi-so-gae. Japanese: ji-ko-shou-kai. Same concept, same characters, predictable sound shift.

The Full Self-Introduction Script

Japanese (日本語)

初めまして。
[Name]と申します。
韓国から来ました。
[Occupation]をしています。
日本語を勉強しています。
どうぞよろしくお願いします。

Korean (한국어)

처음 뵙겠습니다.
[이름]이라고 합니다.
한국에서 왔습니다.
[직업]을 하고 있습니다.
일본어를 공부하고 있습니다.
잘 부탁드립니다.

Line-by-Line Breakdown

Greeting 初めまして hajimemashite "For the first time" (beginning) 처음 뵙겠습니다 Set phrase -- memorize as a unit
Name [Name]と申します [Name] to moushimasu [Name]-[quote] humbly-say [이름]이라고 합니다 と = quotation particle (Korean 라고). 申す = humble form of 言う

Occupation [X]をしています [X] wo shiteimasu [X]-[object] doing-am [X]을 하고 있습니다 している = progressive form (Ch08). Identical structure to Korean ~하고 있다

Closing どうぞよろしくお願いします douzo yoroshiku onegai shimasu please well-[hon]-wish do-[polite] 잘 부탁드립니다 Untranslatable set phrase. Meaning: "Please look favorably upon me"

Elaborative Interrogation

WHY does Japanese use 申します (moushimasu, humble) instead of 言います (iimasu, plain "say") in self-introductions?

Think about this: In Korean, you say ~이라고 합니다 (plain "say") but could use ~이라고 말씀드립니다 (humble). Japanese defaults to the humble form in introductions because self-deprecation is the social norm -- you lower yourself to elevate the listener. This is 謙譲語 (kenjoogo / humble speech), which you will study in depth in Chapter 14.

8B: Small Talk Patterns (世間話 / 일상 대화)

After the self-introduction, you need to keep the conversation going. Small talk in Japanese follows predictable patterns that map beautifully to Korean equivalents.

Weather Talk (天気の話)

Weather is the universal conversation starter. Japanese people use it constantly, just like Koreans.

JapaneseRomajiKoreanUsage
今日は暑いですね kyou wa atsui desu ne 오늘 덥네요 It's hot today, isn't it
今日は寒いですね kyou wa samui desu ne 오늘 춥네요 It's cold today, isn't it
いい天気ですね ii tenki desu ne 날씨 좋네요 Nice weather, isn't it
雨が降っていますね ame ga futteimasu ne 비가 오고 있네요 It's raining, isn't it
蒸し暑いですね mushiatsui desu ne 무덥네요 / 습하네요 It's humid, isn't it
The Magic of ね (ne)

The sentence-ending particle ね works exactly like Korean ~네(요). Both seek agreement, soften statements, and create connection. 暑いですね = 덥네요. The function is a 100% transfer -- even the sound is close: ne/ne!

Compliments and Reactions

JapaneseRomajiKoreanWhen to use
日本語がお上手ですね nihongo ga ojouzu desu ne 일본어 잘하시네요 Complimenting someone's Japanese (you will hear this a lot!)
いいえ、まだまだです iie, madamada desu 아니에요, 아직 멀었어요 Humble deflection -- the expected response
すごいですね！ sugoi desu ne! 대단하네요! That's amazing!
おしゃれですね oshare desu ne 멋지네요 / 세련되네요 That's stylish! (clothes, places)
美味しそう！ oishisou! 맛있겠다! Looks delicious!
Cultural Note: The Compliment-Deflection Dance

When someone says 日本語がお上手ですね, never say はい (yes). Instead, deflect with いいえ、まだまだです (no, I still have a long way to go). This is identical to Korean culture -- when someone says 잘하시네요, you respond 아직 멀었어요. The humility reflex transfers perfectly.

趣味は映画を見ることです shumi wa eiga wo miru koto desu 취미는 영화 보는 거예요 こと = 것 (nominalizer). 映画/영화 -- Cognate

8C: Making Plans (約束する / 약속하다)

Making plans with someone is where real conversation happens. Note how 約束/약속 is yet another cognate -- you already know the word for "making plans/promises"!

SituationJapaneseRomajiKoreanGrammar Note
Are you free Saturday? 土曜日、暇ですか？ doyoubi, hima desu ka? 토요일 시간 있어요? 暇 (hima) = free time. Korean uses 시간 있다 (have time)
Let's go together 一緒に行きましょう issho ni ikimashou 같이 가요 ~ましょう = volitional "let's" (Ch08). 一緒に = 같이
What time? 何時にしましょうか？ nanji ni shimashou ka? 몇 시로 할까요? ~ましょうか = "shall we?" (proposal form)
How about 2 o'clock? 2時はどうですか？ niji wa dou desu ka? 2시는 어때요? ~はどうですか = ~은/는 어때요? (suggestion pattern)
Where shall we meet? どこで会いましょうか？ doko de aimashou ka? 어디서 만날까요? どこで = 어디서 (where-at). 会う = 만나다
At the station 駅で会いましょう eki de aimashou 역에서 만나요 駅/역 -- Cognate
Sounds good! いいですね！ ii desu ne! 좋네요! Agreement -- used exactly like Korean
Sorry, that day is no good すみません、その日はちょっと... sumimasen, sono hi wa chotto... 죄송해요, 그 날은 좀... ちょっと... = 좀... (trailing off = polite refusal)
The Power of ちょっと... (chotto...)

Japanese people rarely say "no" directly. Instead, they trail off with ちょっと... ("a little..."), and the listener understands it means "no." This is identical to Korean 좀... -- both cultures use the same indirect refusal strategy. If you hear ちょっと... followed by silence, that is a "no."

Sample Plan-Making Dialogue

Japanese

A: 土曜日、暇ですか？
B: はい、暇ですよ。
A: 一緒に映画を見ませんか？
B: いいですね！何時にしましょうか？
A: 2時はどうですか？
B: 2時でいいです。駅で会いましょう。
A: じゃ、土曜日に！

Korean

A: 토요일 시간 있어요?
B: 네, 있어요.
A: 같이 영화 볼래요?
B: 좋네요! 몇 시로 할까요?
A: 2시 어때요?
B: 2시 좋아요. 역에서 만나요.
A: 그럼, 토요일에!

Identical dialogue structure. Same number of turns, same flow, same social logic. Only the words differ.

8D: Expressing Opinions (意見を言う / 의견 말하기)

Moving beyond small talk requires expressing what you think, like, and want. These patterns are the backbone of real conversation.

"I think..." Pattern

JapaneseRomajiKoreanGrammar
〜と思います ~to omoimasu 〜라고 생각해요 Quote particle + think verb. Identical structure!
日本語は難しいと思います nihongo wa muzukashii to omoimasu 일본어는 어렵다고 생각해요 Japanese [topic] difficult [quote] think
明日は雨だと思います ashita wa ame da to omoimasu 내일은 비라고 생각해요 Tomorrow [topic] rain [copula] [quote] think
この映画はいいと思います kono eiga wa ii to omoimasu 이 영화는 좋다고 생각해요 This movie [topic] good [quote] think

Likes and Dislikes

PatternJapaneseKoreanNote
I like ~ 〜が好きです 〜을/를 좋아해요 Japanese uses が (subject marker), Korean uses 을/를 (object marker) -- structural difference!
I don't like ~ 〜が好きじゃないです 〜을/를 안 좋아해요 じゃない = negation of copula (Ch08)
I really like ~ 〜が大好きです 〜을/를 엄청 좋아해요 大好き = 大 (big) + 好き (like) = love/really like
I hate ~ 〜が嫌いです 〜을/를 싫어해요 嫌い (kirai) = dislike. Use carefully -- quite strong
Watch Out: Particle Difference with 好き

Korean says: 커피를 좋아해요 (coffee-[object] like)
Japanese says: コーヒーが好きです (coffee-[subject] likeable-is)

This is because Korean 좋아하다 is an action verb ("to like"), but Japanese 好き is a な-adjective ("likeable"). Different grammar, same meaning. This is one of the few places where the particle mapping breaks down. Use が, not を, with 好き.

Wants and Preferences

PatternJapaneseKoreanExample
I want (thing) 〜がほしいです 〜이/가 갖고 싶어요 新しいパソコンがほしいです = 새 컴퓨터가 갖고 싶어요
I want to (verb) 〜たいです 〜고 싶어요 日本に行きたいです = 일본에 가고 싶어요
I don't want to 〜たくないです 〜고 싶지 않아요 帰りたくないです = 돌아가고 싶지 않아요

8E: Apologies and Requests -- The Escalation Ladder

Both Japanese and Korean have a formality spectrum for apologies. The levels map almost perfectly. Understanding when to use which level is as important as knowing the words.

Apology Escalation: Japanese ↔ Korean

LevelJapaneseKoreanWhen to use
Casual ごめん 미안 Close friends, family. Very casual.
Polite casual ごめんなさい 미안해요 Acquaintances, light apologies. Polite but personal.
Standard polite すみません 죄송해요 Strangers, colleagues. Also means "excuse me." Default safe choice.
Formal/Business 申し訳ございません 대단히 죄송합니다 Serious situations, business, to superiors. Deep apology.
Apology formality ladder -- both languages have nearly identical escalation levels. Default to すみません (Level 3) when unsure.

LevelJapaneseKoreanExample

Polite 〜してください 〜해 주세요 ちょっと待ってください = 잠깐만 기다려 주세요
More polite 〜していただけますか 〜해 주시겠어요? 少々お待ちいただけますか = 잠시 기다려 주시겠어요?

8F: Filler Words -- What Makes You Sound Natural (つなぎ言葉)

Textbooks teach you grammar. Filler words teach you how to sound human. These tiny words buy you thinking time, connect ideas, and signal that you are a real conversationalist -- not a phrasebook robot.

"A foreigner who uses えーと and あのう sounds more fluent than one who speaks grammatically perfect sentences with no fillers."

Hesitation and Thinking Fillers

KoreanJapaneseFunctionUsage Note
음... / 그... えーと... / あのう... Stalling for time / "um..." えーと is casual, あのう is slightly more polite. Use constantly!
그래서 だから / それで "So..." / "Therefore..." だから is more direct (can sound pushy). それで is softer and more common in conversation
근데 でも / けど "But..." / "However..." でも starts a sentence. けど ends a clause. Both map to 근데
그리고 それから / あと "And then..." / "Also..." それから is formal, あと is casual. Like Korean 그리고 vs 또
진짜? 本当に？ / マジで？ "Really?!" 本当に is standard. マジで is slang (like 진짜 vs 레알). Use マジで with friends only!

Conversation Reaction Fillers

KoreanJapaneseMeaningWhen to use
아 그래요? あ、そうですか？ "Oh, is that so?" Showing interest. Use frequently as a listener
그렇구나 そうなんだ "I see" / "Is that so" Casual acknowledgment -- like active listening
네네 / 응응 うんうん / はいはい "Uh-huh, uh-huh" Backchanneling -- absolutely essential in Japanese! Nod and say this while someone talks
역시! やっぱり！/ さすが！ "As expected!" / "As I thought!" やっぱり = 역시 (confirmed expectation). さすが = admiration
어쩐지 どうりで "No wonder..." When something finally makes sense
Elaborative Interrogation

WHY is backchanneling (相槌/あいづち) more important in Japanese than in Korean?

Both cultures value active listening, but Japanese speakers use backchanneling more frequently. In Korean, you can listen silently and nod. In Japanese, if you are silent, the speaker may think you are not listening or disagree. You should say うんうん, はい, そうですか, or なるほど ("I see") every few sentences. Think of it as a verbal nod -- the speaker needs constant confirmation that you are following along.

Putting It All Together: A Natural-Sounding Exchange

Notice the fillers (highlighted in bold) that make this sound like real speech:

Natural Japanese

A: あのう、週末は何をしましたか？
B: えーと、友達と映画を見ました。
A: あ、そうですか。どんな映画ですか？
B: ホラー映画です。けど、あまり怖くなかったです。
A: 本当に？私はホラーが好きじゃないです。
B: そうなんだ。じゃ、今度コメディを見ましょう！

Natural Korean

A: 있잖아, 주말에 뭐 했어요?
B: 음, 친구랑 영화 봤어요.
A: 아, 그래요? 어떤 영화예요?
B: 공포 영화예요. 근데 별로 안 무서웠어요.
A: 진짜? 저는 공포영화 안 좋아해요.
B: 그렇구나. 그럼 다음에 코미디 봐요!
