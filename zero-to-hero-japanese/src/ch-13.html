Chapter 13Honorifics and Politeness — Mapping 존댓말 to 敬語

Memory Technique Spotlight: Pragmatic Schema + Role-Play Encoding

Honorific language is deeply tied to social context. By mentally role-playing scenarios (meeting your boss's boss, answering a customer call, switching registers mid-conversation), you build pragmatic schemas that activate the right honorific level automatically — just like you already do in Korean.

In Ch05, you used ください and お願いします without knowing these are keigo. In Ch08, you used 申します in your self-introduction. You've been using honorifics — now let's understand the full system.

"Korean speakers already live inside an honorific system. You know when to switch registers, who outranks whom, and how a single verb ending reshapes a social situation. Japanese keigo is a different map of the same territory."

Your Korean Advantage

You already grasp the logic: raise others, lower yourself, adjust endings by context. In Korean, you shift between 해체, 해요체, 하십시오체 automatically. In Japanese, you shift between 普通体, 丁寧語, 尊敬語/謙譲語. The instinct is the same — only the forms differ.

The four-layer honorific map. Korean and Japanese share the same conceptual architecture — casual, polite, honorific (elevate others), humble (lower self).
Elaborative Interrogation

WHY does Korean merge 높임말 into verb endings (시) while Japanese uses entirely different verbs (召し上がる vs 食べる)?

Korean embeds honorifics agglutinatively: 먹다 → 드시다, 가다 → 가시다 — the 시 infix is productive and predictable. Japanese does this too with the お～になる pattern, but it also maintains a set of completely irregular "special honorific verbs" that have no morphological relationship to the plain form. This is the area where your Korean intuition will not save you — you must simply memorize the pairs.

These are the irregular keigo verbs that do not follow a predictable pattern. You must memorize them as fixed pairs, just as you memorized 먹다 → 드시다 as a child. The table below maps plain → sonkeigo (respect) → kenjougo (humble) → Korean parallels.

Notice the Pattern

いらっしゃる is the Swiss Army knife of sonkeigo — it covers 行く, 来る, and いる. Similarly, いただく covers both 食べる and もらう in humble form. Context determines the meaning, just as 계시다 in Korean covers both "being" and "staying" in honorific form.

Mnemonic: The Big Five

Prioritize these five sonkeigo/kenjougo pairs first. They appear in virtually every business or formal interaction:
1. いらっしゃる / 参る — being, going, coming
2. おっしゃる / 申す — speaking
3. 召し上がる / いただく — eating, receiving
4. ご覧になる / 拝見する — seeing
5. なさる / いたす — doing

Sonkeigo elevates the listener's actions; kenjougo lowers the speaker's actions. The effect is the same: increasing the distance of respect. Korean uses the same two-axis system.

Section 3: The お/ご Prefix System

Japanese has a systematic way to make nouns and adjectives more polite by adding お or ご before them. The rule is based on the origin of the word:

お + 和語 (native Japanese words)

Words inherited from ancient Japanese (和語/やまとことば) take お:

はし → おはしchopsticks
水 → お水water
名前 → お名前name
茶 → お茶tea
金 → お金money
手紙 → お手紙letter
体 → お体body/health

ご + 漢語 (Sino-Japanese words)

Words borrowed from Chinese (漢語/かんご) take ご:

住所 → ご住所address
連絡 → ご連絡contact
家族 → ご家族family
意見 → ご意見opinion
案内 → ご案内guidance
質問 → ご質問question
注文 → ご注文order
Korean Parallel: Inherently Honorific Words

Korean does not use a prefix system like お/ご. Instead, Korean has entirely separate honorific nouns:
밥 → 진지 (meal), 말 → 말씀 (words), 나이 → 연세 (age), 이름 → 성함 (name), 집 → 댁 (house).
Japanese is more systematic: attach お/ご and the word is polished. Korean replaces the word entirely.

The お/ご + Verb Patterns (Productive Keigo)

Beyond memorizing special verb pairs, you can generate keigo for any verb using these formulas:

Elaborative Interrogation

WHY does Japanese split honorific prefixes by word origin (お for 和語, ご for 漢語) while Korean has no such split?

Japanese maintains a deep distinction between native words (和語) and Chinese-borrowed words (漢語) throughout its grammar and phonology. The お/ご split is a surface reflection of this etymological awareness. Korean absorbed 한자어 so thoroughly that the native/borrowed distinction does not drive grammatical rules in the same way. This is another instance of Japanese preserving historical layering that Korean has smoothed over.

Section 5: Common Keigo Mistakes Foreigners Make

Even advanced learners make these errors. Your Korean background protects you from some but not all.

Mistake Double Keigo (二重敬語)

✗ お召し上がりになる

✓ 召し上がる

召し上がる is already sonkeigo. Adding お～になる on top creates double honorifics. In Korean, you would never say *드시시다.

Mistake Sonkeigo for Your Own Actions

✗ 私がいらっしゃいます

✓ 私が参ります

いらっしゃる elevates the subject. Using it for yourself is like saying *제가 가시겠습니다 in Korean — grammatically absurd.

Mistake Honorifying Your Own Company

✗ 私の会社の社長がおっしゃいました

✓ 弊社の社長が申しました

When talking to outsiders, everyone in your company — including your boss — is "your side" and gets humble language. Same as Korean: 저희 사장님이 말씀하셨습니다 becomes 저희 사장이 말씀드렸습니다 in formal external contexts.

Mistake Using ございます Everywhere

✗ それはすごくございます

✓ それは素晴らしゅうございます (or simply 素晴らしいです)

ございます is the keigo form of ある, not a universal politeness booster. It has strict grammatical rules for what it can attach to.

Mistake Mixing Levels Mid-Sentence

✗ 先生が来たんですが、もう帰りになりました

✓ 先生がいらっしゃったんですが、もうお帰りになりました

Keep the register consistent throughout the sentence. If you use sonkeigo for one verb about the same person, use it for all.

Mistake Over-Polite in Casual Settings

Using keigo with close friends or younger family members sounds stiff and creates distance. Just as speaking 하십시오체 to your best friend in Korean would feel bizarre, using 召し上がりますか with a close friend is equally unnatural. Read the room.
