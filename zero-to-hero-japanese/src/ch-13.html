Chapter 13Native Japanese Vocabulary -- Words Korean Can't Help With

Memory Technique Spotlight: Keyword Method + Vivid Imagery

Native Japanese vocabulary (和語) doesn't transfer from Korean, so you need powerful mnemonics. For each word: find a keyword that sounds like the Japanese, create a bizarre/vivid/emotional image linking it to the meaning, then place it in your Park memory palace. The more outrageous the image, the better you'll remember.

Palace Assignment: A Familiar Park (공원)

Choose a park you know well. The entrance gate, bench, fountain, playground, trees, path, bridge, pond, flower bed, statue — each becomes a station for native vocabulary words, anchored with vivid keyword images.

In Ch05, you learned 水 (みず, water) and 美味しい (おいしい, delicious) as survival phrases. Now let's systematically build your 和語 (wago) vocabulary across all categories.

"Sino-vocabulary (漢語) is your shortcut. 和語 is where shortcuts end and memorization begins. Every word in this chapter must be drilled, mnemonicked, and made automatic."
No Romaji From Here

Starting from Chapter 11, romaji is gone. All readings are in hiragana. By now you should be reading kana fluently. If you are still reaching for romaji, go back to Chapters 02-03 for a refresher before continuing.

Why 和語 is Hard for Korean Speakers

Your Korean advantage with 한자어 (Sino-vocabulary) is enormous -- but it covers formal, academic, and compound words. The everyday, emotional, concrete vocabulary of Japanese comes from 和語 -- native Japanese words that share zero overlap with Korean. 물 (water) has nothing to do with みず. 먹다 (eat) is unrelated to たべる. This chapter gives you keyword mnemonics for every single word so brute memorization becomes structured recall.

Elaborative Interrogation

WHY are body part words almost always 和語 rather than 漢語?

Body parts are among the oldest, most basic vocabulary in any language. They were named long before Chinese influence reached Japan (6th century). These words predate kanji itself. The kanji characters were later assigned to existing native words (訓読み/くんよみ), not the other way around. This is why body vocabulary resists Sino-replacement -- it is too deeply embedded in daily speech.

Elaborative Interrogation

WHY do 日 (day/sun) and 火 (fire) share the same native reading ひ?

In ancient Japanese, the sun and fire were conceptually linked as sources of light and heat. The word ひ originally referred to the fundamental concept of luminous heat. The kanji were assigned later from Chinese to distinguish the written forms, but the spoken word retained its unified origin. Context always makes the meaning clear.

Onomatopoeia -- オノマトペ

Japanese has one of the richest onomatopoeia systems in the world. Unlike English, Japanese uses sound-symbolic words not just for sounds but for emotions, textures, states, and movements. These are essential for natural-sounding Japanese.

Three Types of Onomatopoeia

擬音語 (ぎおんご) -- mimics actual sounds (ドンドン = banging)
擬態語 (ぎたいご) -- describes states/feelings (ワクワク = excited)
擬容語 (ぎようご) -- describes appearances/movements (キラキラ = sparkling)
Korean has a similar system (의성어/의태어), so the concept transfers, but the specific words do not.

Japanese onomatopoeia: three categories. The concept exists in Korean, but every word is different.

Elaborative Interrogation

WHY is Japanese onomatopoeia mostly written in katakana?

Katakana is used for emphasis, foreignness, and sound effects. Onomatopoeia in manga, ads, and casual writing uses katakana to make words visually "pop." However, the softer, gentler onomatopoeia (like しとしと, gentle rain) is often written in hiragana to convey that softness visually. The script choice itself carries meaning. Korean does not have this dual-script tool.
