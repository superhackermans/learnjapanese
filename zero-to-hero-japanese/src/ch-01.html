The Grammar Transfer Map

Korean and Japanese share the same grammatical DNA. Look at this sentence:

Korean

나는 사과를 먹는다

na-neun sagwa-reul meongneunda

I (topic) apple (object) eat

Japanese

私はりんごを食べる

watashi wa ringo wo taberu

I (topic) apple (object) eat

100% structural match. Same word order, same particle functions, same verb-final position. The ONLY difference is the words themselves.

What Doesn't Transfer — Where to Invest Your Time

Elaborative Interrogation

WHY does Korean share so much grammar with Japanese but have such different core vocabulary?

Think about this before reading on. The grammar similarity is likely due to deep Altaic-type structural convergence (or shared areal features) over millennia. The vocabulary divergence is because Korean native words (순우리말) and Japanese native words (和語/wago) evolved independently. Only the borrowed Chinese-origin words (한자어/漢語) overlap — and even those shifted in pronunciation over centuries through different sound changes.

Writing System Difficulty: ★★★★

Three scripts: hiragana (46), katakana (46), kanji (~2,000 for literacy). Hiragana and katakana take days. Kanji is the long game — but your hanja knowledge gives you a massive shortcut (Ch10).

Pronunciation Difficulty: ★★

Japanese is simpler than Korean phonetically: fewer consonants, no 받침, simpler syllable structure. But pitch accent is entirely new, and you'll need to unlearn aspirated consonants (Ch04).

Core Vocabulary Difficulty: ★★★★★

The biggest investment. 和語 (wago) — native Japanese words for everyday things — are completely different from Korean. 水 (mizu) = water, 食べる (taberu) = eat. Your hanja helps with formal/academic words, not daily life (Ch12).

Verb Conjugation Forms Difficulty: ★★★

Same system (agglutinative suffixes), different forms. You know how to drive — now the controls are rearranged (Ch07).

Adjective System Difficulty: ★★

Japanese splits adjectives into two types (い-adjectives and な-adjectives) that conjugate differently. Korean doesn't make this distinction the same way.

Cultural Pragmatics Difficulty: ★★

Similar spirit, different execution. Korean 눈치 maps to Japanese 空気を読む, but the specifics of when to bow, what to say, and how to decline differ (Ch17).

The Sound Correspondence Shortcut

Korean and Japanese on'yomi readings follow predictable sound rules (covered in depth in Ch10-11). For example: Korean ㅎ often corresponds to Japanese k — 학 (hak) → 学 (gaku). Once you learn the patterns, you can predict Japanese readings from Korean.

The Three-Way Demonstrative System

This is one of the most satisfying transfers. Korean and Japanese have an identical three-way pointing system:

DistanceKoreanJapaneseMeaning
Near speaker이것 (igeos)これ (kore)This one
Near listener그것 (geugeos)それ (sore)That one (near you)
Far from both저것 (jeogeos)あれ (are)That one (over there)
Question어느 것 (eoneu geos)どれ (dore)Which one?

100% transfer — The concept, the three-way split, and the question form all map perfectly. Only the words differ.

Interactive Exercises

Think Before You Read

WHY does Korean have a three-way consonant distinction (ㄱ/ㅋ/ㄲ) while Japanese only has two-way (k/g)?

Consider: Japanese never developed the tense/aspirated distinction. This means Japanese pronunciation is actually simpler for you — you're downshifting, not upshifting. You'll explore this in Chapter 04.
