Chapter 14Reading Real Japanese — Mixed-Script Reading Strategies

Memory Technique Spotlight: Extensive Reading + Incidental Acquisition

Reading Japanese text exposes you to vocabulary and grammar in natural context — the most powerful form of incidental learning. Your Korean kanji knowledge lets you read at a much higher level than your "official" Japanese level, creating a virtuous cycle of comprehension and acquisition.

You know hiragana, katakana, and a foundation of kanji. Now it is time to read real Japanese — the kind that appears on websites, in newspapers, on signs, and in manga. Real Japanese mixes all three scripts in a single sentence, and learning to parse that mix is a distinct skill.

"Reading Japanese is not decoding one script. It is navigating three scripts simultaneously, each carrying a different signal. Your Korean hanja instinct is the compass."

Section 1: Seeing the Three Scripts at Work

Every Japanese sentence is a tapestry of three scripts. Each script plays a specific role. Let's color-code a real sentence to see the pattern:

東京の天気は今日もとても暑くて、アイスコーヒーを三つも飲んでしまいました。

Kanji — meaning-bearing content words Hiragana — grammar, particles, conjugation endings Katakana — loanwords, foreign names, emphasis

Translation: The weather in Tokyo is very hot today too, and I ended up drinking three iced coffees.

Korean parallel: 도쿄의 날씨는 오늘도 매우 더워서, 아이스커피를 세 잔이나 마셔버렸습니다.

The Script-Function Rule

Kanji = content (nouns, verb/adjective roots). Hiragana = grammar glue (particles, endings, function words). Katakana = foreign imports and special emphasis. This division is consistent across virtually all Japanese text. Once you internalize it, your eyes learn to extract meaning from kanji first, then fill in grammar from the hiragana — exactly how your Korean hanja instinct already works.

Script Functions at a Glance

ScriptPrimary RoleExamplesKorean Parallel
漢字 Content words: nouns, verb/adjective stems 食べる、学校、美しい 한자어 content words (학교, 경제)
ひらがな Particles, conjugation, function words, furigana は、を、の、ている、から 조사 and 어미 (은/는, 을/를, -고)
カタカナ Loanwords, foreign names, onomatopoeia, emphasis コンピューター、パリ、ガタガタ 외래어 (컴퓨터, 파리)
The three-script reading flow. Start with kanji for meaning, then parse grammar through hiragana, and decode loanwords in katakana.

Section 2: Reading Strategies by Media Type

Different media types present Japanese in distinct ways. Each has its own conventions, difficulty level, and reading strategies.

A. Newspapers — NHK Easy News (NHKやさしいにほんのニュース)

Why Start Here

NHK Easy News (NHK NEWS WEB EASY) rewrites real news articles using simplified grammar, limited kanji, and furigana over every kanji. It is the single best free resource for bridging the gap between textbook Japanese and real-world reading.

Characteristics:

  • Furigana on all kanji
  • Simplified grammar (N4-N3 level)
  • Short paragraphs (3-5 sentences)
  • Audio reading available
  • Vocabulary definitions on hover

Korean Advantage:

  • News vocabulary is heavily Sino — your 한자어 shines
  • Formal sentence endings (ました、ています) are predictable
  • Topic structure mirrors Korean news writing
  • 경제 = 経済, 정부 = 政府, 대통령 = 大統領
Newspaper Reading Strategy

Step 1: Read the headline — it is mostly kanji. Use your hanja knowledge to predict the topic.
Step 2: Skim for kanji clusters — these are the key nouns and verbs. Guess the gist.
Step 3: Read hiragana to fill in the grammar. Pay attention to particles and verb endings.
Step 4: Check any katakana words — they are usually English loanwords you already know.

B. Manga — Speech Patterns and Sound Effects

Why Manga is Uniquely Useful

Manga gives you natural spoken Japanese — casual forms, contractions, slang, gendered speech, and emotional expression — in a context where images support comprehension. For Korean speakers, manga parallels the way Korean webtoons (웹툰) use casual speech.

Elaborative Interrogation

WHY are manga sound effects written in katakana rather than hiragana?

Think about it: katakana has angular, sharp strokes that visually convey impact. Hiragana is round and soft. The visual form of katakana itself carries a sense of sharpness, suddenness, and foreignness — perfect for onomatopoeia that represents sounds breaking into a scene. This is a design choice, not a grammatical rule. The same word can appear in hiragana in prose but switches to katakana in manga for visual punch.

Digital Japanese

Section 3: The Korean "Meaning-First" Strategy

As a Korean speaker with hanja knowledge, you have a reading superpower that English speakers do not: you can extract meaning from kanji clusters before understanding a single grammar point. This is your meaning-first strategy.

Demo: Reading a Sentence You've Never Studied

日本政府は新しい経済政策を発表しました。

Step 1: Extract Kanji Clusters

  • 日本 → 일본 = Japan
  • 政府 → 정부 = Government
  • 経済 → 경제 = Economy
  • 政策 → 정책 = Policy
  • 発表 → 발표 = Announcement

Step 2: Get the Gist

Japan + Government + Economy + Policy + Announcement
→ "The Japanese government announced an economic policy."

Step 3: Fill Grammar

は = topic marker, 新しい = new, を = object marker, しました = did (past polite)

Full meaning: The Japanese government announced a new economic policy. — You got 80% of this from kanji/hanja alone.

Elaborative Interrogation

WHY does this meaning-first strategy work better for Korean speakers than for Chinese speakers?

Chinese speakers can also read kanji, but their grammar is SVO — completely different from Japanese SOV. Korean speakers get the double advantage: kanji meaning extraction plus grammatical structure that maps 1:1 onto Japanese. When you see 政府は…を発表しました, the は/を/しました pattern is identical to 정부는…을/를 발표했습니다. Chinese speakers cannot make this grammar transfer.

Section 4: Useful Reading Tools

The right tools accelerate your reading development dramatically. Here are the essentials:

Free Dictionaries

  • Jisho.org — English-Japanese dictionary with kanji lookup by radical, stroke, or drawing
  • Weblio — Japanese-Japanese definitions (monolingual, for intermediate+)
  • Naver 일본어 사전 — Japanese-Korean dictionary, perfect for Korean speakers
  • NAVER Papago — JP↔KR machine translation (better than EN for your purposes)

Free Browser Extensions

  • Yomitan (formerly Yomichan) — hover over any Japanese text to see reading, meaning, pitch accent
  • 10ten Japanese Reader — similar hover-to-read functionality
  • rikaikun — lightweight alternative for Chrome

Install one of these and every Japanese webpage becomes a learning environment.

Varies Reading Apps

  • NHK NEWS WEB EASY — simplified news with furigana (free)
  • Satori Reader — graded reading with grammar notes (paid)
  • Tadoku — free graded readers (Level 0-5)
  • BookLive / Amazon.co.jp — Japanese ebooks and manga

Tip Korean-Specific Strategy

  • Use Naver 사전 over English dictionaries — JP↔KR definitions are more precise for you
  • Look up unknown kanji by their hanja reading first
  • Read Japanese news alongside KBS/MBC Korean news on the same topic to leverage parallel content
  • Set your phone to Japanese — daily exposure through familiar interfaces

Section 5: Practice — NHK Easy News Guided Analysis

Let's walk through a realistic NHK Easy News-style paragraph together. Read it, then work through the guided analysis below.

NHK Easy News style passage (simplified)

日本で外国人の観光客がとても増えています。去年は約三千万人が日本に来ました。政府は二〇三〇年までに六千万人にしたいと言っています。しかし、観光客が増えると、ホテルが足りなくなったり、電車が混んだりする問題もあります。

Guided Analysis

Step 1: Kanji Cluster Extraction (meaning-first)

KanjiKorean HanjaMeaning
外国人외국인 (外國人)Foreigner
観光客관광객 (觀光客)Tourist
増える증(增) = increaseTo increase
去年거년/작년 (去年)Last year
約약 (約)Approximately
三千万人삼천만 명30 million people
政府정부 (政府)Government
問題문제 (問題)Problem

Step 2: Grammar Notes

  • 増えています — ている form = ongoing state. "증가하고 있습니다"
  • 約三千万人が…来ました — past polite. "약 3천만 명이 왔습니다"
  • ～にしたいと言っています — want to make it ~, (they) are saying. "~으로 하고 싶다고 말하고 있습니다"
  • しかし — however. "그러나"
  • ～たり…たりする — listing actions (among other things). "~하기도 하고 ~하기도 하다"

Step 3: Full Translation

Foreign tourists are increasing a lot in Japan. About 30 million people came to Japan last year. The government says it wants to reach 60 million by 2030. However, when tourists increase, there are also problems like hotels running out and trains getting crowded.

Reading Difficulty Ladder

Level 1: NHK Easy News, children's books, graded readers (N5-N4)
Level 2: Regular NHK News, simple manga, blog posts (N3)
Level 3: Newspaper articles, light novels, business emails (N2)
Level 4: Academic papers, literary fiction, legal documents (N1)
Start at Level 1 even if it feels easy. The goal is volume, not difficulty.
